# zk payment channels: field report

> **Note (2026-07-14):** the project's protocol has moved to the
> nullifier-chain design (`PROTOCOL.md`); this report's
> channel-per-gateway application framing predates that move. The
> literature findings themselves stand.

Synthesis of six verified literature-review angles (zk-api-credits, BOLT/zkChannels, hub/multi-hop privacy, ecash/credit tokens, state channels/capital efficiency, formal verification), July 2026. All load-bearing claims below survived adversarial verification against primary sources; corrections from verification passes are applied. Residual uncertainties are listed at the end of each deep dive and in "Open problems / unverified."

Application context: a reputation-gated Tor onion-service egress fleet. Members prove Semaphore membership in zk; an RLN-style nullifier over (secret, epoch) rate-limits per member. Design question: per-request payment across N mutually distrusting gateways without any party linking requests to members. Candidate shapes: (a) a distributed spent set shared by all gateways, (b) a zk payment channel per member-gateway pair.

## TLDR

- The claim that ZK API Usage Credits (Crapis & Buterin, ethresear.ch, Feb 2026) is "fundamentally a zk payment channel bound to one recipient" is confirmed by the primary sources: it is structurally BOLT's (Green & Miers, CCS 2017) unidirectional/bidirectional channel with RLN detect-and-slash substituted for revocation-token punishment, and a thread commenter (sergei-tikhomirov, ex-Lightning) makes the channel mapping almost verbatim (https://ethresear.ch/t/zk-api-usage-credits-llms-and-beyond/24104, https://acmccs.github.io/papers/p473-greenA.pdf).
- Both candidate shapes already exist in mature form: shape (a) "distributed spent set" is deployed as Fedimint (BFT-replicated spent list, https://github.com/fedimint/fedimint) and Nym zk-nym (quorum-maintained global Bloom filter, https://nym.com/docs/network/cryptography/zk-nym/zk-nym-overview); shape (b) is specified as IETF draft-schlesinger-cfrg-act (Anonymous Credit Tokens) and as the zkChannels protocol spec (https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/draft-schlesinger-cfrg-act/, https://boltlabs.tech/userfiles/media/boltlabs.tech/zkchannels-protocol-spec-v0.3.2.pdf).
- For this fleet's profile (near-uniform request cost, roaming members, N mutually distrusting gateways whose threat model is the gateways themselves), the literature weighs heavily toward shape (a): RLN slashing makes an eventually-consistent spent set economically safe (double-spend is detected at reconciliation and priced by the deposit D), while shape (b) fragments capital N ways, fragments the anonymity set per gateway, and hands each gateway BOLT's documented abort-attack lever (https://acmccs.github.io/papers/p473-greenA.pdf §1.4).
- Anonymity sets are always smaller than the clientele: in every hub/channel system verified (TumbleBit, A2L, BOLT, zkChannels) the set is "concurrent, protocol-compatible payments in an epoch," shrinks under aborts, and erodes across epochs via intersection attacks (https://www.ndss-symposium.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/09/ndss201701-3HeilmanPaper.pdf).
- Nothing in the anonymous-channel line ever reached production (BOLT never shipped on Zcash, libzkchannels archived Feb 2023 as proof-of-concept, https://github.com/boltlabs-inc/libzkchannels), and no machine-checked proof of payment unlinkability exists for any channel construction — every privacy proof in this literature is pen-and-paper, and one flagship result (A2L, S&P 2021) had a definitional gap found a year later (https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3548606.3560637).

## What "zk payment channel" actually means

Three distinct objects get called this, and the differences decide the design question.

**1. The BOLT/zkChannels object (the canonical one).** A per-pair escrowed state machine between a customer and one merchant. Each payment: the customer proves in zero knowledge that it holds a validly signed current state with sufficient balance, obtains a blind signature on the decremented state, and hands over a revocation token/secret for the old state. The merchant learns, verbatim from the paper: "no information beyond the fact that a valid payment (of some known positive or negative value) has occurred on a channel that is open with them" (https://acmccs.github.io/papers/p473-greenA.pdf §1.2). Key properties: payments on one channel are mutually unlinkable *to the recipient itself*; the amount is visible; the recipient is pseudonymous and identified by one long-lived keypair; the anonymity set is "all customers with whom the given merchant has a channel open" (zkChannels spec, https://boltlabs.tech/userfiles/media/boltlabs.tech/zkchannels-protocol-spec-v0.3.2.pdf ch.1); double-spend is punished, not prevented — closing on a revoked state forfeits the entire channel balance to the merchant. Note precisely what is anonymous: spends are unlinkable within a pseudonymous channel; channel open/close endpoints are known, and anonymity additionally requires anonymously funded channels ("if channels are funded with anonymized capital" — via a Zcash-style chain *or* any mixing that adds sufficient anonymity to a non-anonymous currency, per the paper's footnote).

**2. The keyed-verification credit object (ACT, ARC).** No chain, no escrow, no dispute game. The issuer signs a hidden (nullifier, balance) token; a spend reveals only a fresh nullifier plus a zk proof of a valid signed balance covering the spend, and the issuer blindly re-issues change (https://www.ietf.org/archive/id/draft-schlesinger-cfrg-act-00.html). Because verification requires the issuer's secret key, **issuer = verifier by construction**: this is a channel's state-update loop with the collateral replaced by "the issuer was already paid at issuance." Double-spend is *prevented* online against the issuer's nullifier DB rather than punished.

**3. The ZK API Usage Credits object.** Deposit D (+ burnable policy stake S) into an on-chain Merkle-tree contract; per request, prove membership, prove the solvency invariant "(i + 1) · C_max ≤ D + R" over a strictly increasing ticket index i and server-signed refund total R, and emit RLN algebra with the ticket index in the role RLN's epoch plays: a = Hash(k, i), x = Hash(M), y = k + a·x, Nullifier = Hash(a). The server keeps a local "Spent Tickets" nullifier DB; reuse of an index with a different message yields two points on the same line, k is recovered, and D is slashed on-chain by anyone (https://ethresear.ch/t/zk-api-usage-credits-llms-and-beyond/24104).

**Novel or rediscovery? Precisely: a rediscovery of the BOLT shape with two genuinely new moves.** The channel skeleton is not new — deposit-as-channel-open, monotone state counter, provider-countersigned balance updates (refund tickets), unlinkable-within-the-deposit-set payer — all of that is BOLT 2016/zkChannels 2021, and sergei-tikhomirov states the mapping in-thread: "we can view the payment mechanics here as a payment channel... The provider must be sure that at any time it can claim on-chain the fair share of the user's deposit" (https://ethresear.ch/t/zk-api-usage-credits-llms-and-beyond/24104). MicahZoltu supplies the differentiator from a plain state channel: "A state channel would certainly be simpler, but it would still correlate requests with each other." What is new: (i) replacing revocation-token punishment (which requires the merchant to watch a chain during dispute windows) with RLN's Shamir-line secret leakage, so double-spend punishment is cryptographic-economic and claimable by anyone, with no watchtower; (ii) the refund-ticket mechanism for variable-cost metering under a solvency invariant, motivated by LLM cost variance — Vitalik: "I would estimate with no refunds my overhead would be like 100x... I need to have a >$5 budget to cover the max possible size, but on average each request costs like a cent," following an "infinite-variance Levy-ish distribution" (same thread). Also genuinely thoughtful: the dual stake, with D claimable via the RLN math and S burnable-but-not-claimable via slashPolicyStake() to kill the false-accusation profit motive. There is prior art for detect-and-reveal double-spend economics outside channels — Chaum-Fiat-Naor offline ecash (https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/0-387-34799-2_25), Compact E-Cash's serial numbers + identity extraction (https://eprint.iacr.org/2005/060), and Nirvana's randomness-reusable threshold encryption revealing double-spenders (Madhusudan, Sedaghat, Jovanovic, Preneel, ePrint 2022/872, https://eprint.iacr.org/2022/872; follow-up "Reusable, Instant and Private Payment Guarantees," Madhusudan, Sedaghat, Tiwari, Cong, Preneel, ACISP 2023, https://eprint.iacr.org/2023/583) — cited in-thread as such. The construction is single-provider by architecture (singular Server throughout, one refund-signing key, one local spent DB, no discussion anywhere in the thread of multiple providers sharing a deposit or nullifier set), though the post never declares this as an explicit limitation.

**Versus ecash:** blind-signature ecash (Chaum https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-1-4757-0602-4_18; Cashu https://github.com/cashubtc/nuts/blob/main/00.md; Taler https://www.taler.net/papers/taler2016space.pdf) makes bearer tokens that roam across verifiers (if publicly verifiable) or across the mint's front-ends, at the price of a spent set someone must maintain. Channels internalize the spent set into pairwise state and eliminate shared infrastructure, at the price of binding all value to one counterparty. BOLT's own §1.3 contains both shapes and rejected bare ecash only for on-chain succinctness of closure — an objection that does not apply off-chain.

## Deep dives

### 1. ZK API Usage Credits (and PrivateX402)

The construction, verified against the thread print view (https://ethresear.ch/t/zk-api-usage-credits-llms-and-beyond/24104, byline "Davide Crapis and Vitalik Buterin," Feb 11 2026): registration deposits Total = D + S against an identity commitment Hash(k) in an on-chain Merkle tree. D is RLN stake, claimable by anyone presenting the recovered secret after a double-signal; S is policy stake, burnable but not claimable by the server ("slashed (burned), but not claimed") via slashPolicyStake(), deliberately killing the false-accusation profit motive. Per request: zk proof (post says ZK-STARK) of Merkle membership, refund summation, solvency (i+1)·C_max ≤ D + R, and RLN components over the ticket index instead of the epoch. The server's double-spend check, verbatim: "If the Nullifier exists with a different x (Message), the user tried to spend the same ticket on two different requests" — solve for k, slash.

Refunds are the load-bearing complication, because LLM cost is wildly variable (Vitalik's 100x-overhead argument, quoted above). Variant 1 accumulates signed refund tickets proven in-circuit; variant 2 keeps a homomorphically encrypted running total E(R), which omarespejel showed is linkable across settlements when static (simulation gist, https://gist.github.com/omarespejel/c3f4f2aa12b1de10467601d77d0e6232 — his ~96% re-linkage figure is his own unreviewed simulation), after which dcrapis updated the spec (https://hackmd.io/3da7PaYmTqmNTTwqxVidRg, reported via thread comments, not fetched directly) to require re-randomization with proof of equivalence and retracted the v2 parallelization claim.

Thread-established open wounds, all verified present in the thread: (a) deposit-edge linkability — dbrizz: the deposit "is a public on-chain transaction linking the user's address (and identity, via exchange KYC trails) to the contract"; proposed fix is shielded/Privacy-Pools-style funding; (b) traffic-analysis relinking via token counts/TTFT/latency — WGlynn: "the server sees token counts, latency, timing, and prompt structure. That's a richer fingerprint than the wallet address itself"; (c) refund trust: disputing an under-reported C_actual deanonymizes; (d) the self-slash race — sergei-tikhomirov: "what if the user makes 9 request, and then deliberately double-signs and tries to slash its own deposit?" — a user who has consumed unclaimed service can race the provider to its own deposit; unresolved in the thread (last visible post drstone, Mar 21 2026); (e) the provider's settlement cadence (when it sweeps earned deposit) is genuinely underspecified in the post.

PrivateX402 (McMenamin & Grigor, Nethermind, Feb 18 2026, https://ethresear.ch/t/privatex402-privacy-preserving-payment-channels-for-multi-agent-ai-systems/24151) is the multi-recipient cousin and the wrong kind of channel for a gateway-adversary threat model: one deposit + 50% collateral funds N channels via Leaf = H(SessionKey, AgentAddress, MaxSpend); "the contract stores only the root and total funding — individual allocations remain private." But privacy targets chain observers and other recipients, not the payee: payments are plain EIP-191 signed cumulative receipts under a per-channel session key, so each recipient links all of one user's requests into one profile. Proofs are currently a mock TEE ("TEE now, ZK later"). It explicitly positions against per-payment-zk designs: "With RLN, a user interacting with N independent providers generally needs N deposits/commitments. PrivateX402 covers N agents with a single deposit and keeps payments proof-free."

Prior art cited in-thread: Nirvana (Madhusudan, Sedaghat, Jovanovic, Preneel, ePrint 2022/872) — anonymous zero-confirmation payment guarantees where double-spend reveals identity via randomness-reusable threshold encryption — and its follow-up (Madhusudan, Sedaghat, Tiwari, Cong, Preneel, ACISP 2023, ePrint 2023/583, doi 10.1007/978-3-031-35486-1_25). A commenter's claims that this lineage is commercialized as 4mica.xyz "live on testnet" and a partial Zeko rollup demo (https://github.com/zeko-labs/developer_demos/tree/main/agent_coordination_protocol-financial_intelligence) remain unverified.

### 2. BOLT and zkChannels

BOLT (Green & Miers, CCS 2017, https://acmccs.github.io/papers/p473-greenA.pdf; eprint 2016/701) gives three constructions. **Unidirectional**: repurposed compact e-cash — wallet (k, sk, B), PRF-derived serial numbers s = F_k(i), double-spend tags whose algebra reveals the cheater's key, exculpability so the merchant cannot frame customers, plus a succinct-closure trick (encrypted chain of B pre-committed spend transactions). This is the direct ancestor of "anonymous credits + spent set." BOLT rejected bare posted-coin ecash *only* for on-chain succinctness of closure (§1.3) — an objection void off-chain. **Bidirectional** (what zkChannels productized): per payment, prove in ZK a merchant signature on the current state with balance ≥ ε, blindly obtain a refund token on B−ε (CL-style signatures), hand over a revocation token for the old state; closing on a revoked state lets the merchant prove it and take the entire channel balance. Punishment, not prevention — someone must watch the chain during dispute windows. Payment amounts are visible to the merchant. **Third-party**: A pays B through one untrusted intermediary that "cannot link transactions to individual users, nor — surprisingly — can they learn the amount being paid." The paper extends the construction to multiple intermediaries (§4.3) but is explicit that the advantages "do not fully generalize": beyond one intermediary, at least one intermediary inevitably learns payment values. So the privacy-relevant configuration is single-hub, though multi-hop is mechanically possible.

The privacy guarantee is deliberately asymmetric and its anonymity set is explicit: unlinkability of payments vs the merchant, set = customers with open channels at that merchant, and only "if channels are funded with anonymized capital" (Zcash-style chain or sufficient external mixing). The paper warns that a merchant handing each payer unique channel parameters reduces "the anonymity set of a single person." And §1.4's abort attacks are the sharpest transferable result: "by aborting during protocol execution, the merchant can place the customer in a state where she is unable to conduct future transactions... (1) The merchant can arbitrarily reduce the anonymity set by (even temporarily) evicting other users through induced aborts. (2) The merchant may link a user to a repeating sequence of transactions by aborting the user in the middle of the sequence."

zkChannels (Bolt Labs spec v0.3.2, DRAFT, 2021-08-29, https://boltlabs.tech/userfiles/media/boltlabs.tech/zkchannels-protocol-spec-v0.3.2.pdf) reframes BOLT over an abstract arbiter, uses Pointcheval-Sanders signatures, revocation locks/secrets, a dedicated unlinking protocol severing the first payment from the identifiable channel open, and states the asymmetry crisply: "the customer's anonymity set for a payment is the set of all customers with whom the given merchant has a channel open"; "the merchant learns nothing else about the payment except the amount"; dispute → "the merchant receives the entire channel balance"; both parties online or watchtowered. Bitcoin support replaces ZK with maliciously secure garbled-circuit 2PC at 7–9 s per payment (https://github.com/boltlabs-inc/libzkchannels).

Deployment reality: zero. ECC blogged interest in 2016 and never shipped (https://electriccoin.co/blog/bolt-private-payment-channels/); the Tezos deployment was "next year" as of July 2020 with no completion evidence (https://medium.com/boltlabs/introducing-zkchannels-on-tezos-c365830b9efd); libzkchannels was archived Feb 28 2023, self-described "not suitable for production." No published third-party cryptanalysis of BOLT was found; the only documented weaknesses are the authors' own abort attacks. Treat the spec as a design reference, not software. Serialized-payments-per-channel (one current closing-authorization signature at a time, so bursty concurrency needs batching or multiple channels) is an inference from the state-chain design that neither document explicitly discusses.

### 3. Hub and multi-hop privacy (TumbleBit → A2L → BlindHub/Accio; AMHL)

TumbleBit (Heilman, AlShenibr, Baldimtsi, Scafuro, Goldberg, NDSS 2017, https://www.ndss-symposium.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/09/ndss201701-3HeilmanPaper.pdf): unidirectional payment hub via RSA puzzles + cut-and-choose, fixed 1-unit denominations, on-chain escrows both legs, one cash-out per escrow. Its anonymity definition is the field's reference: all "compatible interaction multi-graphs" equally likely, giving k-anonymity where "if k payments successfully completed during an epoch, the anonymity set is of size k" — not the clientele. The paper itself flags payer-vs-payee asymmetry (payees do better), abort-induced shrinkage (k → k−1), and cross-epoch intersection attacks. Cost: 327 KB per payment.

A2L (Tairi, Moreno-Sanchez, Maffei, S&P 2021, https://eprint.iacr.org/2019/589.pdf) rebuilds this from adaptor signatures + randomizable puzzles: hub decrypts its own trapdoor value blinded twice (r_A·r_B·k), so it cannot correlate legs; ~33x less bandwidth than TumbleBit; needs only signatures + timelocks on-chain; adds pre-paid randomizable registration tokens against griefing (registration details sourced from the authors' Oakland slides, not the paper). **The cautionary tale**: Glaeser, Maffei, Malavolta, Moreno-Sánchez, Tairi, Thyagarajan (CCS 2022, https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3548606.3560637) "identify a gap in their formal model and substantiate the issue by showing two concrete counterexamples: we show how to construct two encryption schemes that satisfy their definitions but lead to a completely insecure system" — definitional counterexamples, not a demonstrated coin theft against deployed A2L; do not upgrade the claim. They define Blind Conditional Signatures as the right abstraction and repair the protocol as A2L+ (game-based) and A2L-UC (UC-secure, significantly costlier). Build A2L+, never the 2021 spec.

BlindHub (Qin et al., S&P 2023, https://eprint.iacr.org/2022/1735) removes the fixed-denomination requirement (amount was a linking tag) via blind adaptor signatures; Accio (Ge et al., CCS 2023, https://eprint.iacr.org/2023/1326) gets variable amounts without NIZKs by hiding state on only the payee side and never pre-locking tumbler coins, which kills griefing by construction.

AMHL (Malavolta, Moreno-Sanchez, Schneidewind, Kate, Maffei, NDSS 2019, https://eprint.iacr.org/2018/472.pdf) covers paths rather than hubs: Lightning's single-hash HTLC lets all path intermediaries collude-link, and worse admits the wormhole attack ("steal the fees from honest intermediaries in the same payment path"); AMHL randomizes the lock per hop, achieving relationship anonymity vs colluding intermediaries separated by an honest node, at <100 ms and <500 B per operation. The paper claims "the Lightning Network developers have implemented our ECDSA-based AMHLs into their PCN" — that is the authors' 2019 assertion; production Lightning still runs HTLCs with PTLC rollout ongoing, so do not repeat it as current fact.

Structural point for the fleet: the PCH topology hides who-pays-whom through a third party that is neither payer nor payee. In the egress fleet the gateway *is* the payee, so the right ancestor for the per-pair shape is BOLT, not TumbleBit; the hub literature becomes relevant only if payments route through a fleet treasury/hub (see Application).

### 4. Ecash and credit tokens (Cashu, Fedimint, Taler, Privacy Pass, ACT/ARC, Nym)

Cashu (https://github.com/cashubtc/nuts/blob/main/00.md): blind Diffie-Hellman key exchange — B_ = Y + rG, mint returns C_ = kB_, unblind to C = kY; redemption checks k·hash_to_curve(x) == C and the spent-secret list. (The customary attribution of this scheme to David Wagner's 1996 cypherpunks post is not in NUT-00 itself.) Keyed verification → only the issuing mint can verify; the mint is a full custodian. Unlinkable change via swap; powers-of-two denominations are the anonymity knob.

Fedimint (https://github.com/fedimint/fedimint) is candidate shape (a) productized: same Chaumian bearer model, but issuance is threshold blind signatures across a t-of-n guardian federation and the spent list is BFT-consensus state. Guardians see the spent set but cannot link redemptions to issuance; privacy holds against any sub-threshold coalition. GNU Taler (v1.0 in 2025, https://www.taler.net/en/news/2025-01.html) is the most complete treatment of ecash's annoying edges — notably the refresh protocol for unlinkable change, because naive change is a linkage hole (https://www.taler.net/papers/taler2016space.pdf).

Privacy Pass (RFCs 9576/9577/9578) standardizes single-use unlinkable tokens in privately verifiable (VOPRF) and publicly verifiable (blind RSA) flavors, plus the attester/issuer/origin trust split; batched issuance (draft-ietf-privacypass-batched-tokens) amortizes "a book of tickets." Public verifiability removes the issuing key from the request path but **not** the shared spent set — replay defense still needs one per origin. ARC (draft-ietf-privacypass-arc-crypto, Yun/Wood/Faz-Hernandez) is one keyed-verification credential presentable up to N times, "Each presentation is pairwise unlinkable... nor can the server link a presentation to the preceding credential issuance flow." ACT (draft-schlesinger-cfrg-act, Schlesinger & Katz, Google) is ARC with money semantics: BBS-style hidden balance, per-spend nullifier checked against the issuer's DB (online prevention), blind re-issuance of range-proofed change. The draft names "rate limiting and API credits" as applications; a claim circulating that the draft says Cloudflare and Google are exploring it for AI-agent rate-limiting is **not in the draft** and should be treated as unsourced. ACT/ARC being keyed-verification means issuer = verifier: they are, precisely, zk payment channels bound to one recipient with prevention instead of punishment — the same object as ZK API Usage Credits under a different double-spend philosophy.

Nym zk-nym (https://nym.com/docs/network/cryptography/zk-nym/zk-nym-overview) is the closest deployed system to the fleet's exact problem: pay once → threshold subset of quorum members (from a DKG) blind-sign partial signatures → user assembles a "ticketbook" of unlinkable, rerandomizable tickets (Coconut lineage, https://arxiv.org/abs/1802.07344, plus offline-ecash elements) → spend tickets at any gateway. Double-spend: "This ticket is later presented to the Quorum by the Gateway that collected it" (framed in the docs as reward accounting) and quorum members maintain "the global Bloom Filter for double-spend protection." Whether gateways query the filter synchronously before accepting, the reconciliation cadence, and the penalty for a caught double-spender are all undocumented — the "bounded race window" reading is inference. The Tor-payment ancestry (PAR 2008, https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-540-70630-4_14; XPay; "Paying the Guard") repeatedly died on the mint round trip and the double-spend DB at spend time, which is exactly what Nym's deferred settlement engineers around.

### 5. Capital efficiency (factories, virtual channels, Lightning engineering, economics)

The uniform verdict: the on-chain funding *event* is amortizable; the *collateral* is not. LND's BatchOpenChannel opens N channels in one transaction but N separate escrows (https://lightning.engineering/api-docs/api/lnd/lightning/batch-open-channel/index.html); dual funding/splicing/liquidity ads (https://bitcoinops.org/en/topics/dual-funding/) are a whole market that exists to price per-counterparty collateral.

Channel factories (Burchert, Decker, Wattenhofer, Royal Society Open Science 2018, https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsos.180089): one n-of-n multisig hook funds many subchannels, funds "can be moved between channels with just a few messages inside this collaborating group" — the one construction where capital moves between counterparties off-chain. Savings are blockchain space (50% at 3 parties, 90% at 20 users/100 channels), not aggregate collateral; reallocation needs unanimous n-of-n signatures, so one stalled member freezes flexibility. Wrong tool for mutually distrusting gateways.

Perun (Dziembowski, Eckey, Faust, Malinowski, IEEE S&P 2019, https://eprint.iacr.org/2017/635.pdf): virtual payment channels — two parties with ledger channels to a common hub open a direct channel off-chain, hub uninvolved per payment, UC-proven. One member deposit at the hub, N virtual channels carved off it; the hub fronts matching collateral (exact collateral rule and "financial neutrality" are sourced from the Perun 2.0 whitepaper https://perun.network/wp-content/uploads/Perun2.0.pdf, not the S&P paper text). The catch for this fleet: the hub sees which virtual channels each ledger channel opens — member↔gateway pairings, exactly the metadata the zk layer hides. And the Perun lineage of "rooted" virtual channels admits the Domino attack (Aumayr, Moreno-Sanchez, Kate, Maffei, NDSS 2023, https://www.ndss-symposium.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/02/ndss2023_f370_paper.pdf): a malicious party opening a VC over honest base channels "can close the whole path of underlying PCs and bring them on-chain"; Donner fixes it, reducing dispute transactions from linear in path length to one and storage from logarithmic to constant.

Economics: Guasoni, Huberman, Shikhelman (Management Science 70(6), 2024; online Nov 2023; https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/mnsc.2022.01664) prove channels beat on-chain only above a payment-frequency threshold; "Unidirectional channels costs grow with the square-root of payment rates, while symmetric bidirectional channels with their cubic root." (The oft-quoted r^(-1/2)/r^(-1/3) collateral-vs-interest-rate exponents are not in the abstract and remain unverified against the paywalled full text.) Sprites (Miller, Bentov, Bakshi, Kumaresan, McCorry, FC 2019, https://arxiv.org/abs/1702.05812) cuts multi-hop collateral time from Θ(ℓΔ) to O(ℓ+Δ). Ark (https://docs.arklabs.xyz/ark.pdf) shares one UTXO across users with unilateral exits — solves capital, not anonymity; the operator sees everything.

The fleet-relevant reading of Guasoni et al.: egress traffic per member per *specific* gateway is per-member frequency divided by N — the low-frequency regime where a dedicated channel does not pay for itself, before even counting anonymity costs.

### 6. Formal verification landscape

What exists: Lightning has the full stack. Kiayias & Thyfronitis Litos (IEEE CSF 2020, https://eprint.iacr.org/2019/778.pdf) — "for the first time, a full formalisation and security analysis of the lightning network in the (global) universal composition setting," proving balance security with guarantees that "depend on the properties of the underlying ledger and the frequent availability of the protocol participants" (the watchtower assumption, formalized). Machine-checked: Fabiański, Stefański, Thyfronitis Litos (arXiv 2503.07200) prove in Why3, "for the first time... a machine checkable proof" that a simplified LN safeguards honest users' funds in every scenario. Model-checked: Grundmann & Hartenstein (arXiv 2505.15568) make TLA+ checking feasible via two proven refinements (timed-automata-style time abstraction; decomposition so single channels and multi-hop payments are checked separately), verify up to 4-hop payments with 2 concurrent payments, and conclude the current LN spec is secure. BitML (Bartoletti & Zunino, CCS 2018, https://eprint.iacr.org/2018/122.pdf) shows the calculus + automated liquidity checking + Agda mechanization pipeline (https://github.com/omelkonian/formal-bitml). The UC channel line (Perun, generalized channels https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-92075-3_22, Bitcoin-compatible virtual channels https://eprint.iacr.org/2020/554.pdf) is pen-and-paper UC throughout.

What is greenfield: **no machine-checked proof of payment unlinkability exists for any channel construction** (absence claim from systematic search across TLA+/Why3/Coq/Isabelle/Lean × channels × privacy; not provably exhaustive). Every privacy proof in this literature — Bolt's game-based definitions, Fulgor/Rayo's UC PCN privacy and its privacy-vs-concurrency impossibility (https://eprint.iacr.org/2017/820.pdf), AMHL's relationship anonymity, A2L+/BCS, BlindHub — is pen-and-paper. And the A2L episode proves the definitions themselves are where the danger is: a peer-reviewed S&P 2021 privacy protocol had a model gap admitting "completely insecure" instantiations, found a year later.

Prover guidance for a fleet credit protocol, ranked by cost/value: (i) TLA+ model of the credit state machine (issue, spend, nullifier check, reconcile, slash, dispute) — Grundmann-Hartenstein showed channel-level protocol logic is model-checkable at realistic scope, and this protocol is simpler than Lightning (unidirectional, no HTLC routing); days of effort, catches the state-machine bugs where implementations actually break. (ii) Mechanized balance/fund-safety in Why3 following arXiv 2503.07200, or crypto-level proofs in SSProve (Coq state-separating proofs, already mechanizes sigma-protocol ZK, https://eprint.iacr.org/2021/397) or CryptHOL (Isabelle game-hopping + Constructive Cryptography, https://eprint.iacr.org/2017/753.pdf); EasyCrypt remains the workhorse for big game-hopping. (iii) Frontier: machine-checked unlinkability — nobody has done it; SSProve is best-aligned, CryptHOL the alternative. Lean 4 is attractive long-term because VCV-io (https://eprint.iacr.org/2026/899.pdf) and ArkLib (https://lean-lang.org/use-cases/arklib/) are verifying exactly the SNARK components (Fiat-Shamir, sum-check, FRI) a Semaphore-adjacent stack rests on, but its game-based protocol layer is too young to plan a channel-privacy proof on this year; mathlib has no crypto games.

## Application: channel-per-gateway for the egress fleet

**The collapse that decides the question.** ZK API Usage Credits' one-recipient binding is two engineering facts, not doctrine: refund tickets signed by one provider key, and one provider's local spent DB. Egress requests are near-uniform cost, so Vitalik's entire refund rationale (infinite-variance LLM costs) does not apply to "fetch one URL." Drop refunds, price every ticket flat at C, and the construction collapses to: prove membership + (i+1)·C ≤ D + per-index RLN nullifier. That is a strict superset of the deployed Semaphore+RLN stack — one new circuit constraint (the counter inequality) and a second nullifier domain (index for spend, keep epoch for rate). WGlynn's quantized-pricing suggestion in-thread is the same design; fixed denominations also keep the fleet out of BlindHub territory (amount-as-linking-tag).

**Why shape (a) wins for this fleet.** RLN slashing makes the distributed spent set eventually-consistent-safe: a cross-gateway double-spend (same nullifier, different x at gateways A and B) is detected at reconciliation, k is recovered, D is slashed — the async window is *priced by D*, not trusted away. This is exactly the trade Chaumian ecash cannot make (double-spend there is free absent a synchronous spent set) and exactly the trade Nym makes operationally with its deferred Bloom-filter reconciliation. Gateways gossip (Nullifier, x, y) tuples on whatever lag they tolerate; the tuples are anonymous, so gossip leaks only global volume/timing. BOLT's history endorses this: its unidirectional construction *is* compact e-cash in an escrow, and the paper rejected the bare spent-set design only for on-chain closure succinctness (§1.3) — irrelevant when the gateways replicate the set off-chain and there is no channel to close. What (a) must import from e-cash: exculpability (a gateway must not be able to frame a member as a double-spender; RLN gives this since forging a double-signal requires knowing k — BOLT needed dedicated tag algebra for the same property) and the deliberate consistency choice between Fedimint-style synchronous BFT (prevention, consensus latency on the request path) and Nym-style deferred reconciliation (fast, bounded race insured by D).

**Why shape (b) loses, concretely.** Three independent costs at N gateways:
1. *Capital fragmentation*: N deposits per member, each sized to worst-case spend at that gateway, in a regime (per-member frequency / N) where Guasoni et al.'s frequency threshold says a channel is not worth its lockup (https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/mnsc.2022.01664). Batch open amortizes only the funding transaction, never the escrow — and funding N channels from one wallet at onboarding links the member to the gateway set unless funding is itself anonymized (BOLT's anonymized-capital requirement, resurfacing as dbrizz's deposit-KYC warning in the credits thread).
2. *Anonymity-set partition*: payment anonymity at gateway G becomes "members with an open channel at G" (zkChannels' own definition). Lazily opened channels partition the membership exactly the way Semaphore currently doesn't; fixing that requires every member pre-provisioned at every gateway, multiplying cost 1 again.
3. *The abort lever*: BOLT §1.4 hands each merchant an active traffic-analysis primitive — evict users via induced aborts to shrink the set, or abort mid-sequence to link a user across transactions. Our adversary IS the gateway. The paper's mitigation (scan for premature closures, abort below an anonymity threshold) assumes a public channel registry; here the registry is the adversary's private state.
   Also: zkChannels-style channels serialize payments per channel (inference from the state-chain design), awkward for bursty Tor traffic; and PrivateX402, the one cheap multi-recipient channel design, is disqualified outright because its recipient sees a stable session key — every request linkable by the gateway into one profile.

**The middle path worth costing (from the state-channels angle).** One zk channel per member to a *fleet treasury* (single escrow, O(1) in N), with A2L+-style blind conditional signatures disbursing to gateways and gateways settling against the treasury in batch. This keeps member capital constant, keeps gateways unable to link, and confines consistency to hub↔gateway settlement between pseudonymous businesses. It buys hard per-gateway settlement at the price of a highly available treasury and A2L+'s epoch-k anonymity accounting (k = concurrent payments, shrinking under aborts, eroding under intersection). Only reach for it if gateways refuse nullifier gossip entirely.

**Design imports regardless of shape.**
- *Dual stake, wholesale*: D slashed cryptographically for double-spends; S burnable-not-claimable by gateways for abuse-policy violations — fits an egress fleet's abuse problem better than it fits LLM billing.
- *Engineer around the self-slash race* (unresolved in the credits thread): gateways claim earned deposit frequently, or serve only up to a small unclaimed balance per epoch, because an anonymous member racing its own slash after consuming unclaimed service is rational.
- *Fund D through a shielded pool / Privacy-Pools-style mechanism* or the anonymity set is fiction at the deposit edge.
- *Nullifier hygiene*: the payment (index) nullifier and the membership (epoch) nullifier must not share derivations, or payment becomes a cross-epoch linking handle for the membership identity.
- *Epoch/traffic discipline*: the effective anonymity set is per-epoch completed requests per gateway, not the member roster; coarse epochs, flat pricing, and padding/decoys at low-utilization gateways are the levers. Tor circuits give more cover than an HTTPS API, but destination/response-size/timing fingerprints (WGlynn's point) still bound payment unlinkability from above.
- *Threshold issuance option*: if the fleet wants prevention rather than detection at the base layer, Fedimint/Coconut-style t-of-n issuance with a BFT spent set is the deployed pattern (Nym being the closest full match: threshold issuance + shared spent structure above the gateways — a hybrid of both candidate shapes).

## Formal verification: what exists, what is greenfield, which prover

Covered in deep dive 6; summary for planning:

- **Exists and is reusable**: UC balance-security methodology (Kiayias-Litos), TLA+ decomposition refinements (Grundmann-Hartenstein), Why3 fund-safety mechanization (Fabiański et al.), vetted privacy vocabulary (relationship anonymity from AMHL, blindness/unlinkability from BCS/A2L+, PCN privacy + the concurrency-privacy impossibility from Fulgor/Rayo).
- **Greenfield**: machine-checked unlinkability for any channel/credit construction — no instance found in the literature. A distributed-spent-set consistency proof for mutually distrusting replicas would also be new: note the Kiayias-Litos structure reappears in disguise there, since a shared spent set is a small consensus layer whose liveness/consistency carries the load the ledger carries in channel proofs — if gateways can partition or equivocate, double-spend safety degrades exactly as channel safety degrades with a bad ledger. RLN slashing is what converts that degradation from "unsafe" to "priced."
- **Prover choice**: TLA+ first (days, catches state-machine bugs, protocol simpler than the already-checked Lightning); Why3 or SSProve/EasyCrypt second for fund-safety/crypto games; SSProve if attempting the frontier unlinkability mechanization (it already has sigma-protocol ZK); watch Lean's VCV-io/ArkLib for the SNARK-component layer but do not schedule protocol-privacy proofs on Lean this year.
- **Process lesson (A2L)**: instantiate a published, repaired abstraction (BCS/A2L+) or budget external review of the *definitions*, not just the code. Privacy definitions for payment protocols have failed peer review after acceptance.

## Open problems

1. **Cross-gateway spent-set consistency with priced (not prevented) divergence.** Specify the gossip protocol for (Nullifier, x, y) tuples, the reconciliation cadence, and prove the bound: maximum extractable double-spend value before detection ≤ f(gossip lag, per-epoch rate limit) < D. No literature covers an eventually-consistent spent set backed by RLN slashing; Nym's deployed analogue leaves cadence and penalties undocumented.
2. **The self-slash race.** A member who has consumed service whose payment the gateway hasn't yet claimed can double-sign deliberately and race the gateway to D. Unresolved in the ZK API Credits thread. Candidate mitigations (frequent claims; small unclaimed-balance ceiling per epoch) need adversarial analysis, especially interaction with gossip lag from problem 1.
3. **Deposit-edge anonymity.** The on-chain deposit KYC-links members to the membership set. Integrate shielded/Privacy-Pools-style funding with the Semaphore registration flow such that neither the deposit nor the Merkle insertion is a linking event.
4. **Dual-nullifier circuit design.** One proof carrying both an epoch nullifier (rate) and an index nullifier (spend) over the same secret k, with a proof that the derivations are domain-separated and jointly reveal nothing beyond each alone. The credits post uses index-instead-of-epoch; nobody has specified index-AND-epoch.
5. **Exculpability of the gossip layer.** A gateway must not be able to fabricate a double-spend transcript for a nullifier it saw legitimately. RLN's algebra (forging a second point on the line requires k) plausibly gives this for free — prove it, including collusion of up to N−1 gateways sharing transcripts.
6. **Abort/eviction resistance metrics.** Quantify the BOLT-§1.4-style anonymity damage a malicious gateway can do in the spent-set design (selective serving, delayed reconciliation) versus the channel design (induced aborts), under Tor timing realities. The spent-set design should dominate; measure it.
7. **Machine-checked unlinkability.** First mechanized proof of spend-unlinkability for any credit/channel construction — flat-ticket RLN credits are arguably the smallest such target in the literature (no refunds, no revocation, one inequality). SSProve is the indicated framework.
8. **Settlement cadence specification.** When and how gateways sweep earned deposit is underspecified in the source construction (sergei-tikhomirov's unanswered comment); it interacts with problems 1, 2, and 6.
9. **Intersection-attack budget.** Cross-epoch frequency analysis erodes k-anonymity (TumbleBit's own warning). For a fleet with stable membership, compute the epochs-to-deanonymization curve under realistic usage skew and set epoch coarseness/decoy policy from it.
10. **The treasury-hub fallback, fully costed.** If nullifier gossip is politically impossible: member→treasury zk channel + A2L+/BCS blind disbursement to gateways. Needs: treasury liveness/abort analysis (Domino-style griefing), k-anonymity accounting per settlement epoch, and whether TEE-free proofs are practical at request rate.
