## The constructions

**BOLT's stated privacy guarantee (all three constructions).** Quoting the paper (§1.2, p.474 of the CCS version, https://acmccs.github.io/papers/p473-greenA.pdf): *"Upon receiving a payment from some customer, the merchant learns no information beyond the fact that a valid payment (of some known positive or negative value) has occurred on a channel that is open with them. The network learns only that a channel of some balance has been opened or closed."* Three things to notice. First, this is unlinkability of payments against the merchant, with the anonymity set = the set of customers who currently have a channel open with that merchant. Second, the payment **amount is revealed** to the merchant (hidden only from a single intermediary in the third-party construction). Third, channel open/close is **not** anonymous by itself: the network sees a channel of some balance open and close, and the guarantee only becomes "anonymous" if channels are funded with anonymized capital, e.g. Zcash shielded funds (footnote 2, p.474: unlinkability holds "if channels are funded with anonymized capital"). Privacy is deliberately asymmetric: the merchant is a well-known identity and should publish *one* set of channel parameters for all customers — the paper explicitly warns that a merchant handing each payer unique channel parameters reduces each payer's anonymity set to one (§1.2, p.475).

**Unidirectional construction (this is the "distributed spent set" shape).** It is compact e-cash (Camenisch–Hedwig–Lysyanskaya) repurposed: the customer's wallet is a tuple `(k, sk, B)` where `k` seeds a PRF (Dodis-Yampolskiy) generating up to `B` fixed-value coins, each coin = (serial number `s = F_k(i)`, double-spend tag `T`, ZK proof that the coin is validly derived from a merchant-signed wallet). Double-spending the same coin yields two transcripts sharing serial `s` whose tags combine to **reveal the cheater's key** — detection is after-the-fact and algebraic, not preventive, and the scheme needs *exculpability* so the merchant can't frame a customer with fake double-spends (§1.3, p.475). BOLT's novel contribution here is succinct closure: instead of posting all unspent coins on-chain, the customer pre-commits an encrypted chain of `B` spend transactions at establishment; revealing the decryption key for position `j` unlocks positions `j..B`, letting the merchant verify no further spending happened, in constant on-chain space (§4.1, p.480).

**Bidirectional construction (this is the "zk payment channel" shape, and what zkChannels productized).** Customer and merchant escrow funds against an initial state. The customer holds a *wallet* (commitment to channel ID, balance, keys). Each payment of amount ε is an interactive protocol: (1) prove in ZK knowledge of a merchant signature on the current state with balance ≥ ε, (2) blindly obtain a signed *refund token* for the new balance `B - ε` (signature with efficient protocols, Camenisch–Lysyanskaya style), (3) hand the merchant a *revocation token* for the old state so it can never be used to close (p.476). The merchant never sees which wallet is being updated, only that some valid wallet was decremented by ε. If the customer later closes with a revoked (old) refund token, the merchant posts the matching revocation token as "indisputable evidence" and **the network awards the entire channel balance to the merchant** (p.476). Whole-balance slashing is the double-spend deterrent; there is no prevention, only punishment, so the merchant must watch the chain (or hire a watchtower) during dispute windows.

**Third-party payments (single hub).** Two bidirectional channels through one untrusted intermediary I: A pays B by decrementing the A–I channel and incrementing the I–B channel atomically (conditional payment), such that I "cannot link transactions to individual users, nor ... learn the amount being paid," and even a compromised I can't steal in-flight funds (p.477). The paper is explicit that this does **not** generalize past one hop: in any chain with more than one intermediary, some intermediary learns the payment amount, and at least one channel has intermediaries at both endpoints (§1.2, p.475). BOLT is a hub topology, not a Lightning-style network.

**zkChannels (the successor).** Per the spec (https://boltlabs.tech/userfiles/media/boltlabs.tech/zkchannels-protocol-spec-v0.3.2.pdf, ch.1): the protocol is reframed over an abstract *arbiter* J (in practice a cryptocurrency network) doing escrow + dispute arbitration, so it's chain-agnostic (account- or UTXO-based). The channel is explicitly **asymmetric**: "the merchant is at most pseudonymous and remains identifiable across all channels; the customer is at most pseudonymous during channel establishment and closure, but has the ability to make payments anonymously *as long as they have an open channel with sufficient balance*... the customer's anonymity set for a payment is the set of all customers with whom the given merchant has a channel open." Channel state is a sequence: each state carries a *revocation lock*; a payment = customer commits to new state s′, proves in ZK it's s minus ε with a valid unspent *payment tag* on s, merchant **blindly issues a closing authorization signature** on s′ (Pointcheval-Sanders signatures, spec §2.2), customer reveals the *revocation secret* for s, merchant issues a payment tag on s′. The merchant keeps a database of revocation secrets — "each successful payment results in a revocation secret that allows the merchant to track whether a given state has been spent, but the merchant learns nothing else about the payment except the amount." Closure: mutual close off-network, or unilateral close by either side with a dispute timeout; double-spend during the timeout → merchant crafts a dispute transaction and "receives the entire channel balance" as punishment. Both parties must stay online or designate a watchtower. A dedicated *unlinking protocol* at establishment severs the first payment from the identifiable channel-opening interaction. Bitcoin support replaces the ZK proofs with maliciously secure 2PC (merchant garbles, customer evaluates), at 7–9 s per payment in malicious mode (https://github.com/boltlabs-inc/libzkchannels).

## Relevance to our egress fleet

The claim that shipped with this question — that a ZK-API-usage-credits scheme is "fundamentally a zk payment channel bound to one recipient" — is **confirmed by the primary sources**. Both BOLT bidirectional and zkChannels are structurally exactly that: a per-(member, gateway) escrowed state machine, merchant-identified by a long-lived keypair, where the member proves in ZK "I hold a valid signed state with balance ≥ ε" and gets a blind signature on the decremented state (https://boltlabs.tech/userfiles/media/boltlabs.tech/zkchannels-protocol-spec-v0.3.2.pdf; https://acmccs.github.io/papers/p473-greenA.pdf p.476). One-recipient binding isn't incidental; it's what makes the anonymity set well-defined ("all customers with whom the given merchant has a channel open") and what the paper's warning about per-payer channel parameters protects.

Opinionated mapping to our two candidate shapes:

- **Your option (b) is BOLT-bidirectional/zkChannels.** N mutually distrusting gateways → N channels per member → N escrows/credit balances per member, and N separate anonymity sets. That last part is the real cost, worse than the capital cost: a member's payment anonymity at gateway G is only "members with an open channel at G." If members open channels lazily (only at gateways they use), channel-open patterns partition the membership exactly the way Semaphore membership currently doesn't. You'd need every member to open a channel at every gateway (or via an issuer that pre-provisions all N), which multiplies the pre-funding N-fold.
- **Your option (a) is BOLT-unidirectional's ancestor.** The compact-e-cash core of BOLT §4.1 — PRF-derived serial numbers, ZK proof of a signature on the wallet, double-spend tags — is precisely "anonymous credits + a spent set." BOLT rejected the naive "post spent coins" design **only for on-chain succinctness** (§1.3: "it is not succinct, since closure requires the customer to post all of her unspent coins"). That objection does not apply to us: our "arbiter" is not a blockchain, gateways can gossip/replicate a serial-number spent set off-chain cheaply, and we don't need succinct closure because there's no channel to close. Note also the structural rhyme with what we already run: an RLN nullifier over (secret, epoch) *is* a PRF-derived serial number; a spent-set credit is the same object with a per-coin index instead of a per-epoch index. Option (a) is a smaller conceptual step from our deployed system than option (b).
- **What (a) must import from the e-cash literature that a naive spent set misses:** exculpability (a gateway must not be able to frame a member as a double-spender — BOLT gets this from the tag algebra, §1.3 p.475) and the consistency question across mutually distrusting gateways: with an asynchronous shared spent set, double-spend is detect-after-the-fact, so gateways individually eat the cost of races unless serials are partitioned per gateway or spends are (briefly) synchronized. BOLT-unidirectional's answer — identify-and-punish the double-spender — assumes the member has slashable identity/collateral, which reintroduces linkage unless punishment is itself zk (that's RLN's move: leak the secret via Shamir on rate-limit breach).
- **The abort attacks transfer directly and are the strongest argument against (b).** From the paper (§1.4 "Aborts," p.477): with the bidirectional construction, "by aborting during protocol execution, the merchant can place the customer in a state where she is unable to conduct future transactions," and specifically "(1) the merchant can arbitrarily reduce the anonymity set by (even temporarily) evicting other users through induced aborts. (2) The merchant may link a user to a repeating sequence of transactions by aborting the user in the middle of the sequence." For a *mutually distrusting gateway fleet* whose whole threat model is "gateways try to deanonymize members," handing each gateway a per-member abort lever is handing them an active traffic-analysis primitive. The paper's proposed mitigation (customers scan for premature closures and abort if the anonymity set shrinks below threshold) is workable on a public chain but awkward when the "channel registry" is the gateway's own private state.
- **Also note:** payment amounts are visible to the merchant in both BOLT-bidirectional and zkChannels (spec ch.1: "the merchant learns nothing else about the payment except the amount"). Fine for us if all requests cost 1 credit; a liability if request pricing varies by exit type, since price becomes a fingerprinting dimension within the anonymity set. And zkChannels payments are serialized per channel (a state chain with one closing-authorization signature current at a time), so per-request payment at high concurrency from one member through one gateway needs either request batching or multiple channels — another mark against (b) for a rate-limited-but-bursty workload.
- **Deployment reality check:** nothing here ever reached production. ECC blogged about BOLT for Zcash in 2016 (https://electriccoin.co/blog/bolt-private-payment-channels/) and never shipped it; the Tezos amendment was "next year" as of July 2020 (https://medium.com/boltlabs/introducing-zkchannels-on-tezos-c365830b9efd) and I can find no adoption; libzkchannels is archived (Feb 2023) with an explicit not-production-ready warning (https://github.com/boltlabs-inc/libzkchannels). Treat these as design references, not reusable software. The spec PDF is the best artifact: it's a complete message-level protocol with ideal functionalities you could crib for a member–gateway credit channel if (b) wins anyway.

Bottom line opinion: BOLT's own §1.3 contains both of your candidate shapes, and the paper chose channels over bare e-cash for reasons (on-chain succinctness, blockchain arbitration) that mostly don't bind an off-chain gateway fleet. For N mutually distrusting gateways, the e-cash/spent-set shape (a) preserves one fleet-wide anonymity set and avoids per-gateway abort leverage and N-fold escrow; the channel shape (b) buys you per-gateway balance finality at the price of partitioned anonymity sets, abort attacks, and capital fragmentation. If gateways won't share a spent set at all, (b) is the fallback, and zkChannels is the construction to copy.
