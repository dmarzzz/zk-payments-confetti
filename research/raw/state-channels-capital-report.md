# Generalized state channels & capital efficiency: literature report

Scope: what it costs (in locked capital) for a user to hold one payment channel per server, and which constructions let many channels share one on-chain deposit or one funding event. Researched July 2026.

## The constructions

### Channel factories (Burchert–Decker–Wattenhofer)

A factory is a new layer between the blockchain and channels: a group of n nodes pays into a single n-of-n multisig "hook" transaction on-chain, and above it an off-chain "allocation" layer spends that hook into many ordinary two-party channels among group members ([RSOS 2018](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsos.180089)). Because the allocation is itself off-chain and re-signable by the group, members can tear down one subchannel and redeploy its funds into another subchannel with *no* blockchain interaction — this is the one construction in the literature where capital genuinely moves between counterparties without a new funding event ([RSOS 2018](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsos.180089)).

The measured savings are on-chain footprint: ~50% of blockchain space for 3-party factories and ~90% in a 20-user/100-channel setting ([RSOS 2018](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsos.180089)). Note what is *not* saved: aggregate collateral. Every subchannel is still fully funded from the shared deposit; the factory improves capital *agility* (rebalancing) and amortizes the funding event, not the amount locked.

The costs are severe for mutually distrusting operators. Updating the allocation needs unanimous n-of-n signatures, so one offline or malicious member freezes reallocation (funds remain recoverable via the old allocation, but the factory's flexibility dies). The construction as originally described also leans on eltoo/`SIGHASH_NOINPUT`-style update mechanics that Bitcoin never activated, which is why factories stayed on paper; the modern spiritual successors are covenant/tree designs like Ark and Spark ([Bitcoin Magazine](https://bitcoinmagazine.com/print/ark-and-spark-the-channel-factories-print), [Spark research](https://www.spark.money/research/channel-factories-scaling-lightning)).

### Perun virtual channels and generalized/recursive state channels

Perun's setting is exactly "one hub, many spokes": Alice and Bob each have an on-chain-funded *ledger channel* with intermediary Ingrid, and open a *virtual channel* between themselves built on those two ledger channels. Unlike Lightning routing, Ingrid is not involved in individual payments; she only participates at open, at close, and in disputes ([eprint 2017/635](https://eprint.iacr.org/2017/635.pdf), [S&P 2019 record](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/8835315/)). The virtual channel is funded by locking balance inside both ledger channels: Ingrid effectively fronts collateral in her channel with Bob matching Alice's stake (and vice versa), and the construction is designed to be "financially neutral" for Ingrid — she can never lose money, and a *validity* timeout agreed at open guarantees she gets her collateral back when the virtual channel expires ([Perun 2.0 whitepaper](https://perun.network/wp-content/uploads/Perun2.0.pdf), [Cryptape overview](https://blog.cryptape.com/payment-channel-networks-on-utxo-blockchains)). The capital picture for the *user* is the attractive part: one on-chain deposit into one ledger channel with the hub, then any number of virtual channels to different endpoints carved out of that single deposit, opened and closed purely off-chain. The capital burden shifts to the hub, which must hold balances covering every live virtual channel it intermediates ([Cryptape overview](https://blog.cryptape.com/payment-channel-networks-on-utxo-blockchains)).

The generalizations: Counterfactual showed that within one funded channel you can "install" arbitrarily many applications counterfactually — the on-chain deposit is claimed by whichever signed state is finalized, so N logical channels/apps share one deposit with zero additional on-chain events ([Counterfactual paper](https://github.com/counterfactual/paper), [Horne's explainer](https://medium.com/statechannels/counterfactual-generalized-state-channels-on-ethereum-d38a36d25fc6)). Multi-party Virtual State Channels extends Perun recursively: virtual *state* channels (full contract execution) built over other channels, spanning many parties ([Eurocrypt 2019, eprint 2019/571](https://eprint.iacr.org/2019/571.pdf)). On the Bitcoin side, Bitcoin-Compatible Virtual Channels achieves single-hop virtual channels with adaptor signatures ([S&P 2021](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/b63b0d031561a410dac38dc7cc7a84f9afe73648)), and Elmo makes them recursive and variadic ([eprint 2021/747](https://eprint.iacr.org/2021/747.pdf)).

The security caveat discovered later matters for fleet design: NDSS 2023 showed that "rooted" virtual-channel designs (the Perun-lineage ones) admit the *Domino attack* — a malicious party opening a virtual channel over honest base channels can force the entire underlying path on-chain, a DoS/griefing vector inherent to that design family; Donner restructures multi-hop virtual channels to avoid it and to stop the intermediary-collateral from growing with the path ([NDSS 2023 paper](https://www.ndss-symposium.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/02/ndss2023_f370_paper.pdf)).

### Lightning-engineering answers: batch opens, dual funding, splicing, liquidity ads

Production Lightning already amortizes the *funding event* (though never the collateral): LND's `BatchOpenChannel` opens many single-funded channels atomically in one transaction ([LND API](https://lightning.engineering/api-docs/api/lnd/lightning/batch-open-channel/index.html), [PR #5356](https://github.com/lightningnetwork/lnd/pull/5356)). The dual-funding / interactive-tx protocol lets both peers contribute inputs and outputs to one negotiated funding transaction — including outputs for *other* channels, so multiple channel opens and splices can ride one transaction ([Bitcoin Optech](https://bitcoinops.org/en/topics/dual-funding/), [lightningsplice.com](https://lightningsplice.com/splicing_explained.html)). Splicing resizes a live channel by adding/removing funds without closing it ([Fidelity Digital Assets explainer](https://www.fidelitydigitalassets.com/research-and-insights/introduction-channel-splicing-bitcoins-lightning-network)). Liquidity ads create a protocol-level market where a node advertises rates at which it will lease inbound collateral into your channel ([lnd issue #6569](https://github.com/lightningnetwork/lnd/issues/6569), [LightningNetwork+](https://lightningnetwork.plus/posts/47)). These are the practitioner's toolkit for "N channels off one funding event," and their existence is itself evidence that per-counterparty collateral is the binding cost in practice — an entire liquidity marketplace exists to price it ([Spark comparison](https://www.spark.money/tools/lightning-liquidity-marketplace-comparison)).

The economics literature quantifies the lockup cost. Guasoni–Huberman–Shikhelman derive that a channel beats on-chain settlement only above a payment-frequency threshold; channel cost grows with the square root of payment rate for unidirectional channels (cube root for symmetric bidirectional), and optimal collateral scales as r^(-1/2) (unidirectional) vs r^(-1/3) (bidirectional) in the interest rate r — i.e., the opportunity cost of locked capital is a first-order term in whether a channel makes sense at all ([Management Science 2023](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/mnsc.2022.01664)). Practitioner literature puts the hurdle rate at roughly risk-free yield (~4%+ in 2026) before a routing node breaks even on locked capital ([Clams profitability guide](https://clams.tech/blog/lightning-node-profitability-2026/), [bolt.observer metrics](https://blog.bolt.observer/p/lightning-network-financial-metrics)). Sprites attacks the *duration* dimension of the same cost for multi-hop payments: collateral × time drops from Θ(ℓΔ) to Θ(ℓ+Δ) via a shared on-chain preimage manager ([arXiv 1702.05812](https://arxiv.org/abs/1702.05812)); Payment Trees gets comparable reductions Bitcoin-compatibly ([eprint 2020/1313](https://eprint.iacr.org/2020/1313.pdf)).

### Anonymous per-recipient channels and unlinkable hubs (Bolt, zkChannels, A2L)

Bolt is the citation behind "the ZK API Usage Credits construction is fundamentally a zk payment channel bound to one recipient." A Bolt channel is customer→merchant; within it, each payment proves in zero knowledge possession of a validly signed wallet with sufficient balance and obtains a blind signature on the updated wallet, while the customer signs a *revocation token* on the old wallet — double-spend of an old state is punished because the revocation token lets the merchant claim the channel escrow. The merchant learns only "a valid payment occurred on *some* channel open with me," and on-chain observers see only opens/closes with balances ([CCS 2017 paper](https://acmccs.github.io/papers/p473-greenA.pdf), [the morning paper part II](https://blog.acolyer.org/2017/12/14/bolt-anonymous-payment-channels-for-decentralized-currencies-part-ii/)). Crucially, Bolt's third construction already handles the "pay many recipients without a channel to each" problem: A pays B *through* an intermediary that has channels with both, and the intermediary "learns neither the identity of the participants, nor the amount being transferred," with atomicity enforced by chained revocation/refund tokens ([acolyer part II](https://blog.acolyer.org/2017/12/14/bolt-anonymous-payment-channels-for-decentralized-currencies-part-ii/)). zkChannels re-implements Bolt chain-agnostically (Bitcoin/Tezos) by replacing bespoke scripts with MPC-generated blind ECDSA signatures ([libzkchannels](https://github.com/boltlabs-inc/libzkchannels), [Bolt Labs announcement](https://medium.com/boltlabs/zkchannels-for-bitcoin-f1bbf6e3570e)).

A2L is the hub-topology counterpart: a tumbler forwards payments sender→hub→receiver such that the hub cannot correlate the two sides (anonymous atomic locks built from adaptor signatures + randomizable puzzles), UC-proven, and griefing-resistant via a registration/escrow phase where the sender locks coins before the hub commits collateral ([eprint 2019/589](https://eprint.iacr.org/2019/589.pdf), [S&P 2021](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/9519431/)). Ark is the newest "share one deposit" shape: an operator batches all users into periodic on-chain rounds; users hold VTXOs (pre-signed unilateral exit paths) inside one shared UTXO, need no channels and no liquidity management, at the cost of the server fronting round liquidity and users needing to refresh VTXOs before expiry ([Ark whitepaper](https://docs.arklabs.xyz/ark.pdf), [Bitcoin Optech topic](https://bitcoinops.org/en/topics/ark/), [Second docs](https://docs.second.tech/ark-protocol/intro/)).

## Relevance to our egress fleet

Concrete read, given N mutually distrusting gateways, Tor-grade unlinkability requirements, and RLN-gated members:

1. **The literature's verdict on "one channel per server" is unambiguous: the on-chain event is amortizable, the collateral is not.** Batch opens ([LND](https://lightning.engineering/api-docs/api/lnd/lightning/batch-open-channel/index.html)) and dual funding ([Optech](https://bitcoinops.org/en/topics/dual-funding/)) collapse N funding events into one transaction, but each member still pre-commits N separate escrows sized to worst-case spend per gateway. Guasoni et al. show a channel only pays for itself above a payment-frequency threshold set by the opportunity cost of locked capital ([Mgmt Sci 2023](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/mnsc.2022.01664)). Egress traffic per member per *specific* gateway is exactly the low-frequency, bursty regime where that threshold is not met — members will route through whichever gateway Tor circuits happen to pick, so per-pair frequency is per-member frequency divided by N. A per-pair zk channel (option b, the ZK API Usage Credits shape / Bolt shape) fragments an already small budget N ways. That's the real cost, and it also creates a fingerprint: funding N channels from one wallet at onboarding links the member to the gateway set unless funding is itself anonymized.

2. **Channel factories are the wrong tool here.** The factory's n-of-n update requirement ([Burchert et al.](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsos.180089)) presumes a cooperating group; our gateways are mutually distrusting by design, and a member joining a factory with all N gateways puts its reallocation liveness at the mercy of the *least* available gateway. Factories also make the member↔gateway-set relationship a shared, visible fact within the group. The savings (on-chain space) solve a problem we barely have; the costs (unanimity, group visibility) hit exactly what we care about.

3. **Virtual channels over a hub are the literature's actual answer to our capital question — but they reintroduce a linkage point.** Perun gives the member one deposit (ledger channel with a hub) and free off-chain per-gateway virtual channels ([Perun 2.0](https://perun.network/wp-content/uploads/Perun2.0.pdf)); the hub is financially neutral and can't steal. But the hub sees which virtual channels a given ledger channel opens — i.e., the hub learns member↔gateway pairings and coarse spend, which is precisely the metadata the zk-membership layer exists to hide. If a hub topology is acceptable at all, the relevant literature is the *unlinkable* hub line: Bolt's intermediary construction (hub learns neither identities nor amounts, [acolyer](https://blog.acolyer.org/2017/12/14/bolt-anonymous-payment-channels-for-decentralized-currencies-part-ii/)) and A2L ([eprint 2019/589](https://eprint.iacr.org/2019/589.pdf)). A2L's model maps well: member holds one channel with a payment hub (could be run by the fleet operator or a third party), gateways hold channels with the same hub, and per-request micropayments flow member→hub→gateway with the hub cryptographically unable to link the two legs. Anonymity set = concurrent payers through the hub, which composes naturally with epoch-based RLN batching. Also note the Domino-attack result before adopting Perun-lineage virtual channels in an adversarial setting ([NDSS 2023](https://www.ndss-symposium.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/02/ndss2023_f370_paper.pdf)) — a griefing member can force underlying channels on-chain.

4. **Opinion: for this fleet, the channel literature mostly argues for the spent-set (option a) at low stakes, and for a Bolt/A2L hybrid only if per-request payments must be hard-settled per gateway.** Per-request amounts here are dust; the entire cost structure of channels (escrow sized to max spend, on-chain open/close/dispute, watch requirements) is justified by *disputable balances*, which dust doesn't warrant. Option (a) — blind-signed or zk credit notes with a shared spent set — is economically an ecash mint, which is the degenerate case of Bolt's unidirectional channel (compact ecash inside an escrow, [CCS'17](https://acmccs.github.io/papers/p473-greenA.pdf)) with the escrow collapsed to a prepaid balance. The gateways' real problem with (a) is spent-set consistency across mutually distrusting parties (double-spend across gateways before sync); the channel literature's contribution to that debate is the reminder that Bolt solves double-spend *without any shared state* via revocation-token forfeiture — but only per-recipient, which is what drags the N-escrow capital cost back in. A middle path worth costing out: one zkChannel per member to a *fleet treasury* (single escrow), A2L-style unlinkable disbursement to gateways, gateways settle against treasury in batch. That keeps member capital O(1) in N, keeps gateways from linking requests to members, and confines the consistency problem to hub↔gateway settlement where parties are pseudonymous businesses, not anonymous members.

5. **If off-chain-native is acceptable (no per-request chain settlement at all), Ark-style shared-UTXO batching is the newest relevant shape** — operator-fronted liquidity, zero member-side channel management, unilateral exit ([Ark](https://docs.arklabs.xyz/ark.pdf)) — but its operator sees all activity, so it would need the Bolt/A2L blinding layered on top; it solves capital, not anonymity.
