## The constructions

### Cashu (single Chaumian mint)

Cashu is Wagner's discrete-log variant of Chaumian blinding. The client picks a secret `x`, hashes it to a curve point `Y`, blinds it as `B_ = Y + rG`, and the mint (which holds private key `k`, one per denomination) returns `C_ = kB_` without ever seeing `x`. The client unblinds to `C = kY` and now holds a bearer token `(x, C)` ([NUT-00](https://github.com/cashubtc/nuts/blob/main/00.md)). Because the signature is a plain DH multiplication, verification requires knowing `k`: **only the issuing mint can verify its own tokens**. Tokens are strictly issuer-bound bearer instruments.

Double-spend is handled by pure online prevention: at redemption the mint checks `k·hash_to_curve(x) == C` and that `x` is not in its spent-secret list, then adds `x` to the list ([NUT-00](https://github.com/cashubtc/nuts/blob/main/00.md)). Change works by the swap operation: you hand in proofs worth 8 sats and simultaneously present fresh blinded messages for 5+2+1; the mint signs the new outputs blind, so change is unlinkable. Denominations are powers of two, which is also the anonymity-set knob. Cashu additionally supports spending conditions committed inside the secret (P2PK, and DLEQ/ZK conditions) via NUT-10/11 ([Cashu blog](https://blog.cashu.space/bringing-zero-knowledge-proofs-to-cashu/)). Trust: the mint is a full custodian of the backing funds and can inflate invisibly (mitigations are researched, e.g. [proof-of-liabilities for Chaumian mints](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2306.12783)); the mint learns nothing linking withdraw to spend, but it sees amounts and timing. Real vulnerabilities have occurred at the protocol-engineering layer ([conduition disclosure](https://conduition.io/code/cashu-disclosure/)).

### Fedimint (federated Chaumian mint)

Fedimint keeps the exact Chaumian bearer-token model but replaces the single custodian with a t-of-n guardian federation running BFT consensus; issuance uses threshold blind signatures so no guardian alone can sign, and redemption (spent-list update) goes through consensus ([fedimint/fedimint](https://github.com/fedimint/fedimint), [fedimint.org FAQ](https://fedimint.org/docs/FAQs/TheFederation)). Guardians collectively see the spent-nullifier set but, because of blinding, cannot link any redeemed note to its issuance, so privacy holds even against the guardians ([Spark research overview](https://www.spark.money/research/fedimint-federated-ecash)). Custody of the backing bitcoin is threshold multisig; a colluding quorum can steal funds or inflate, an honest quorum cannot deanonymize.

The structurally important point for us: **Fedimint is "a distributed spent set" productized**. The federation is one logical mint; the spent set is replicated state under consensus among parties who need not trust each other individually, only assume a t-of-n honesty threshold. That is precisely candidate shape (a) from our design question, with the gateways playing guardians.

### GNU Taler (Chaumian ecash with industrial-strength change)

Taler is the most complete treatment of the annoying edges of blind-signature ecash: an "exchange" blind-signs coins; merchants deposit coins with the exchange, which performs **online double-spend detection** and gives merchants instant digital proof against misbehaving customers ([Taler 2016 paper](https://www.taler.net/papers/taler2016space.pdf)). Its signature contribution is the *refresh protocol*: unlinkable change and refunds, so partial spends don't create linkable "tainted change," a problem naive Chaum systems have ([Taler FAQ](https://www.taler.net/en/faq.html), [Taler book](https://www.taler.net/files/taler-book.pdf)). Taler deliberately makes income (merchant side) taxable/visible while keeping payer anonymity; coins are issuer-bound and verified online at the single exchange. It reached v1.0 in 2025 ([release notes](https://www.taler.net/en/news/2025-01.html)).

### Anonymous Credit Tokens (ACT) — and why it is "a zk payment channel bound to one recipient"

ACT ([draft-schlesinger-cfrg-act](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/draft-schlesinger-cfrg-act/)) is a keyed-verification anonymous credential over Ristretto255 with privately verifiable BBS-style signatures. Issuance: the issuer signs a token binding a hidden nullifier `k`, blinding `r`, and a credit balance `c`. Spend: the client reveals *only* the nullifier and a ZK proof (with bit-decomposition range proofs) that it owns a validly signed token whose balance covers the spend; the issuer checks the nullifier against a persistent database (**online prevention**), then blindly re-issues a fresh token on the remaining balance `c - s` as unlinkable change ([draft-00 text](https://www.ietf.org/archive/id/draft-schlesinger-cfrg-act-00.html)). The issuer cannot link issuance to spend or spends to each other; every spend is one round trip to the issuer. There is a Privacy Pass integration draft ([draft-schlesinger-privacypass-act](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/draft-schlesinger-privacypass-act/)), and the CFRG draft states Cloudflare and Google are exploring it for rate-limiting AI agents.

The "payment channel" framing is accurate in a specific sense: because the scheme is keyed-verification, **the issuer and the verifier are necessarily the same party** — no third gateway can check a spend without the issuer's secret key. The client's balance is a piece of rolling state co-maintained with exactly one counterparty, updated per interaction, with change re-issued each round. That is a channel's state-update loop, minus on-chain collateral and minus dispute games (the "collateral" is that the issuer already got paid at issuance). Compare [ZK API Usage Credits](https://ethresear.ch/t/zk-api-usage-credits-llms-and-beyond/24104) (Crapis & Buterin): deposit `D` in a contract, strictly increasing ticket index `i`, RLN-style algebra so reusing an index reveals the secret key and triggers slashing, server-signed refund tickets folded into the solvency proof `(i+1)·C_max ≤ D + R`. Same shape, different double-spend philosophy: ACT *prevents* via an online nullifier DB; ZK API credits *detect and slash* via RLN, which our fleet already runs. Both are explicitly single-provider constructions ([ethresear.ch post](https://ethresear.ch/t/zk-api-usage-credits-llms-and-beyond/24104)). BOLT/zkChannels is the fully-collateralized ancestor: on-chain escrow per customer-merchant pair, merchant can't link payments to a channel, but capital is locked per pair ([BOLT paper](https://eprint.iacr.org/2016/701.pdf), [libzkchannels](https://github.com/boltlabs-inc/libzkchannels)).

### Privacy Pass family (single-use tokens → batched → multi-show ARC)

Base Privacy Pass (RFCs [9576](https://www.ietf.org/rfc/rfc9576.html)/[9577](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/rfc9577/)/[9578](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/rfc9578/)) issues single-use unlinkable tokens in two flavors: privately verifiable (VOPRF — issuer must verify, like Cashu) and publicly verifiable (blind RSA — **anyone with the public key can verify**, but redemption is still one-shot so someone must keep a spent set per token). The architecture deliberately splits *attester* (who rate-limits/authenticates the client) from *issuer* (who signs) from *origin* (who redeems), which is the standardized vocabulary for "who is trusted with what." [Batched issuance](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/draft-ietf-privacypass-batched-tokens/) amortizes buying a book of tokens; [rate-limited issuance](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/draft-ietf-privacypass-rate-limit-tokens/) caps tokens per origin per time window at issuance time. Tokens carry no denomination and no change concept: one token = one unit of access.

ARC ([draft-ietf-privacypass-arc-crypto](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/draft-ietf-privacypass-arc-crypto/)) upgrades this to one keyed-verification credential presentable up to N times, each presentation unlinkable from the others and from issuance — i.e., a prepaid N-request pass with per-presentation nullifiers, again issuer=verifier. ARC is the standards-track object closest to "ACT without balances": counter semantics instead of monetary semantics.

### Nym zk-nym (threshold credentials + offline-ecash tickets, deployed)

zk-nym is the closest deployed system to our exact problem. A user pays (crypto or fiat) via an OrderAPI; funds convert to NYM and land in a contract controlled by a quorum multisig. A threshold subset of Nyx validators (the "NymAPI Quorum") blind-signs partial Coconut-style signatures the user aggregates into a **ticketbook**; the user then derives many small unlinkable **tickets** (bandwidth chunks) offline and spends them at ingress gateways over time ([zk-nym overview](https://nym.com/docs/network/cryptography/zk-nym/zk-nym-overview)). Credentials are rerandomizable, so each showing is fresh and unlinkable ([zk-nym docs](https://nym.com/docs/network/cryptography/zk-nym)). The scheme "incorporates elements of both the Coconut Credential and Offline Ecash schemes" ([docs](https://nym.com/docs/network/cryptography/zk-nym)) — Coconut for threshold issuance ([Coconut, NDSS 2019](https://arxiv.org/abs/1802.07344)), compact/offline ecash lineage for the spendable-ticket structure ([Compact E-Cash](https://eprint.iacr.org/2005/060)).

Double-spend protection is **not** a synchronous per-request check at the gateway: gateways collect tickets and later present them to the Quorum for reward accounting, and Quorum members maintain a **global Bloom filter** of spent tickets ([zk-nym overview](https://nym.com/docs/network/cryptography/zk-nym/zk-nym-overview)). So gateways accept mostly-offline and the system detects/settles centrally — a deliberate latency-vs-enforcement trade. Trust: t-of-n quorum for issuance honesty and for the spent-set; no single gateway or validator can link payment identity to tickets. This is, in deployed form, the hybrid of both our candidate shapes: threshold issuance (so no gateway is the issuer) plus a shared spent structure (the Bloom filter) maintained above the gateways.

### The Tor-payment ancestry

Paying onion routers anonymously per unit of service is a 2008-era literature: [PAR](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-540-70630-4_14) combined bank-issued ecash coins with relay-signed micropayment coins; [XPay](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/221342205_XPay_practical_anonymous_payments_for_tor_routing_and_other_networked_services) and [Paying the Guard](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-662-47854-7_26) followed. The recurring lesson (see also the [Tor Project's incentive-design retrospective](https://blog.torproject.org/two-incentive-designs-tor/)): the bank/mint round trip at spend time and the double-spend database were always the operational bottleneck, which is exactly what Nym's ticketbook + deferred Bloom-filter settlement is engineered around.

## Relevance to our egress fleet

**Our candidate (a) "distributed spent set" is Fedimint/Nym, already built; candidate (b) is ACT, already specified.** This design question has a mapped literature and we should not invent either shape from scratch. Shape (a) = the gateways jointly *are* the mint: threshold blind issuance (Fedimint's guardian model, or Coconut t-of-n like Nym) and a consensus- or quorum-maintained nullifier set. Shape (b) = ACT verbatim: keyed-verification credit balance per member-gateway pair, online nullifier check at that one gateway, blind change re-issuance ([draft-schlesinger-cfrg-act](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/draft-schlesinger-cfrg-act/)). The ethresear.ch characterization checks out: ACT and [ZK API Usage Credits](https://ethresear.ch/t/zk-api-usage-credits-llms-and-beyond/24104) are the same object with different double-spend philosophies (online prevention vs RLN detect-and-slash), and both are explicitly bound to one recipient.

**When ecash dominates the channel.** Blind-signature ecash wins exactly when spends must roam across N verifiers that the member didn't pre-commit to. One ticketbook purchase covers the whole fleet; a member who hits gateway 7 once at 3am doesn't need standing state there. Capital efficiency is the sharp edge: with per-pair channels a member wanting access to N gateways posts N deposits (or ACT balances), almost all idle — BOLT makes this explicit as per-pair escrow ([BOLT](https://eprint.iacr.org/2016/701.pdf)). Ecash also decouples *paying* from *joining*: a new gateway added to the federation is instantly spendable-at with zero member action. The price is the spent set: privately verifiable ecash (Cashu-style) forces every spend through whoever holds the signing key, so for mutually distrusting gateways you need either BFT replication of the nullifier DB (Fedimint — synchronous, prevention, adds consensus latency to the request path) or deferred reconciliation (Nym — Bloom filter above the gateways, small double-spend window a malicious member can race across gateways) ([Fedimint](https://github.com/fedimint/fedimint), [Nym overview](https://nym.com/docs/network/cryptography/zk-nym/zk-nym-overview)).

**When the channel/ACT dominates.** If traffic is gateway-sticky — a member's Tor client typically holds a circuit through one gateway for a session — then per-pair ACT is strictly simpler: no cross-gateway consensus at all, each gateway keeps a private nullifier DB, spends are one local round trip, and change/variable pricing is native (range proofs on balance) ([draft-00](https://www.ietf.org/archive/id/draft-schlesinger-cfrg-act-00.html)). Variable per-request pricing is genuinely awkward in ecash: amounts must be built from fixed denominations, and odd amounts shrink the anonymity set (Taler's refresh protocol exists precisely because naive change is a linkage hole — [Taler](https://www.taler.net/papers/taler2016space.pdf)). Channels also let refunds flow backward trivially; Cashu/Taler handle change well but true refunds-from-merchant are clunkier.

**Opinionated recommendation.** Flat per-request pricing across the fleet, roaming members, N distrusting gateways: that profile points to **threshold-issued ecash tickets, Nym-shaped**. Concretely: gateways (or a quorum superset) run Coconut/threshold-BBS issuance; members buy a ticketbook in one payment event unlinkable to spends; each request presents one fixed-denomination ticket with a nullifier; gateways check a shared nullifier structure. For the shared structure, choose deliberately between Fedimint-style synchronous BFT (prevention, latency cost) and Nym-style deferred Bloom filter (fast, bounded race window) — and note that **we already have RLN**, so the race window is partially insured: a member racing the same ticket across gateways within an epoch can additionally be made to leak its secret RLN-style, converting the ecash gap from prevention to detection-with-slashing, which is exactly the ZK API Credits trick ([ethresear.ch](https://ethresear.ch/t/zk-api-usage-credits-llms-and-beyond/24104)). Reserve per-pair ACT channels as an optimization for gateway-sticky heavy users, not as the base layer — with N gateways and roaming, ACT's issuer=verifier binding recreates the fragmentation problem channels have always had. One more architectural note: publicly verifiable Privacy Pass tokens (blind RSA, [RFC 9578](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/rfc9578/)) let any gateway *verify* without the issuing key, but replay defense still needs the shared spent set, so public verifiability alone does not dissolve problem (a); it only removes the issuance key from the request path. Finally, the ticket-spend and the existing Semaphore/RLN membership proof should be presented together but must not share nullifier derivations, or the payment nullifier becomes a cross-epoch linking handle for the membership identity.
