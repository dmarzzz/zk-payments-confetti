## The constructions

### Kiayias-Litos: Lightning in the UC framework (the reference "balance security" treatment)

The Kiayias-Litos paper is the canonical formal model of a real deployed payment channel network. They model the entire Lightning protocol (channel open, bidirectional updates via revocable commitments, multi-hop HTLC payments, closure and punishment) as a UC protocol running against a *global ledger functionality*, and prove it realizes an ideal payment-network functionality ([eprint 2019/778](https://eprint.iacr.org/2019/778.pdf), [CSF 2020 version](https://conferences.computer.org/sp/pdfs/csf/2020/657200a334.pdf)). The headline property is what the literature now calls **balance security**: an honest party never ends up with less on-chain money than their off-chain balance entitles them to, no matter what all other parties do.

Two aspects matter for anyone reusing this. First, the security guarantee is explicitly *parameterized by ledger properties*: the proof "delineates exactly how the security guarantees of the protocol depend on the properties of the underlying ledger" ([U. Edinburgh entry](https://www.research.ed.ac.uk/en/publications/586c0dbc-a57b-4e1f-9bab-c566ad90a499)) — i.e., how often honest users must watch the chain relative to liveness/finality parameters. Any "gateway settles disputes on some ledger" design inherits this same watchtower-style liveness assumption. Second, this is a pen-and-paper UC proof, hundreds of pages of case analysis; it is precisely the kind of proof the machine-checked follow-ups below were built to de-risk.

The same group's lineage continued into mechanization: Thyfronitis Litos is a co-author of the 2025 Why3 work ([arXiv 2503.07200](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.07200)), which implements a simplified LN and proves, machine-checkably, that honest users' funds are safeguarded in every scenario — per the paper, the first time LN's security goals have machine-checked proofs in an integrated fashion.

### Model checking Lightning: the TLA+ line (Grundmann & Hartenstein)

Grundmann and Hartenstein formalized the Lightning protocol in TLA+ and specified the security property that honest users are guaranteed to retrieve their correct balance ([arXiv 2307.02342](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.02342)). Naive model checking is infeasible — the state space explodes with time, channel state, and concurrent payments. Their 2025 follow-up ([arXiv 2505.15568](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.15568), also a Springer chapter ["Security of the Lightning Network: Model Checking a Stepwise Refinement with TLA+"](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-032-10794-7_16)) makes it feasible with two proven refinement steps: (1) abstracting real time using timed-automata-style arguments, and (2) a compositional decomposition proof that a single payment channel and the multi-hop payment protocol can be model checked *separately*. With that, they check configurations up to four-hop payments with two concurrent payments and conclude the current Lightning specification is secure. An earlier companion piece is ["Verifying Payment Channels with TLA+"](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/361627611_Verifying_Payment_Channels_with_TLA) (2022).

The methodological lesson: channel protocols are small enough that *protocol-logic* safety (no state-machine path lets a counterparty take your money) is tractable for a model checker if you decompose channel-level from network-level reasoning, and keep cryptography symbolic (signatures/hashes as perfect). What TLA+ does not give you is anything about the cryptographic layer — and nothing about privacy, which is a hyperproperty and outside classic TLC-style model checking.

### BitML: contract calculus + automated verification + Agda mechanization

BitML is a process calculus for Bitcoin contracts with both a symbolic semantics and a computational one, connected by a computational soundness result, plus a compiler to actual Bitcoin transactions ([eprint 2018/122](https://eprint.iacr.org/2018/122.pdf), [CCS 2018](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3243734.3243795)). Its toolchain automatically verifies **liquidity** — funds can never be permanently frozen in a contract ([survey](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/blockchain/articles/10.3389/fbloc.2019.00008/full)). BitML's semantics and meta-theory (Chapter 5 of the paper: small-step semantics, honest/adversarial strategies) have been mechanized in Agda in [omelkonian/formal-bitml](https://github.com/omelkonian/formal-bitml).

BitML matters here less as a channel formalization per se (it targets contract-level reasoning; extensions cover cross-chain contracts, e.g. [BitMLx](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2501.17733)) and more as the proof that "domain-specific calculus + automated property checking + proof-assistant mechanization" is a workable pipeline for off-chain money protocols.

### The UC channel-construction line: Perun, state channels, generalized channels, virtual channels

Perun introduced *virtual payment channels*: two parties each with a channel to a common intermediary can open a channel between themselves that doesn't require the intermediary's involvement per payment, with security proven in the UC framework ([S&P 2019](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/8835315/), [slides](https://www.ieee-security.org/TC/SP2019/SP19-Slides-pdfs/Lisa_Eckey_-_02-Lisa_Eckey-PERUN_Virtual_Payment_Hubs.pdf), [perun.network](https://perun.network/)). The same Dziembowski/Faust school produced general state channel networks and then, with Aumayr et al., **generalized channels** — channels that off-chain-execute anything the underlying ledger's script supports, instantiated for Bitcoin via adaptor signatures, with UC proofs ([Asiacrypt 2021](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-92075-3_22), [eprint 2020/476 lineage](https://repositum.tuwien.at/handle/20.500.12708/40215)) — and **Bitcoin-compatible virtual channels**, requiring only signatures + timelocks ([eprint 2020/554](https://eprint.iacr.org/2020/554.pdf), [S&P 2021](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Bitcoin-Compatible-Virtual-Channels-Aumayr-Ersoy/b63b0d031561a410dac38dc7cc7a84f9afe73648)).

The standard security definition across this whole line is UC realization of an ideal channel/network functionality, whose informal core is always the same pair: (i) balance security for honest parties, (ii) liveness/optimistic efficiency. Game-based definitions appear at the primitive level (e.g., adaptor signature unforgeability/witness-extractability), UC at the protocol level. This "game-based primitives, UC protocol" layering is the de facto standard in the field, and [Foundations of Adaptor Signatures](https://eprint.iacr.org/2024/1809.pdf) shows even the primitive-level definitions have needed repair.

### The privacy line: Bolt, Fulgor/AMHL, A2L(+), BlindHub — where unlinkability actually gets formalized

**Bolt** (Green-Miers) is the construction your "ZK API Usage Credits is fundamentally a zk payment channel bound to one recipient" framing is describing. Bolt's observation: even anonymously-funded channels are pairwise, so repeated payments in one channel are inherently linkable; Bolt uses commitments, blind signatures and ZK proofs so that multiple payments on a single channel are unlinkable *to each other* from the merchant/hub's view ([eprint 2016/701](https://eprint.iacr.org/2016/701), [CCS 2017](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3133956.3134093)). Its security definitions are game-based (payment anonymity + balance). The zkChannels project ported the Bolt idea to Bitcoin-family chains ([Tezos Commons explainer](https://medium.com/tezoscommons/a-closer-look-into-zkchannels-90c8a88e90cf)); I found no formal-methods artifact for zkChannels.

**Fulgor/Rayo** gave the first *formal definitions* of PCN privacy, in the UC framework, and proved an impossibility: non-blocking progress for concurrent payments necessarily costs privacy ([eprint 2017/820](https://eprint.iacr.org/2017/820.pdf), [CCS 2017](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3133956.3134096)). **AMHL** then formalized *relationship anonymity* — no user, including an honest-but-curious intermediary, can associate sender and receiver of a payment, defined over an interaction multi-graph — found the wormhole fee-theft attack as a byproduct of the formalism, and gave an ECDSA-compatible construction that LN developers adopted ([eprint 2018/472](https://eprint.iacr.org/2018/472.pdf), [NDSS 2019](https://dblp.org/rec/conf/ndss/MalavoltaMSKM19.html)).

The hub sub-line is directly your shape (member pays through/at a semi-trusted node): **A2L** built an unlinkable payment channel hub from "anonymous atomic locks" ([eprint 2019/589](https://eprint.iacr.org/2019/589.pdf)). Then **Foundations of Coin Mixing Services** showed A2L's formal model had a gap — two counterexample encryption schemes satisfy A2L's definitions yet yield a completely insecure system — and repaired it with the Blind Conditional Signatures abstraction, giving game-based A2L+ and UC-secure A2L-UC (at significant cost) ([eprint 2022/942](https://eprint.iacr.org/2022/942.pdf), [CCS 2022](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3548606.3560637)). **BlindHub** extended hub unlinkability to variable amounts via blind adaptor signatures ([eprint 2022/1735](https://eprint.iacr.org/2022/1735), [S&P 2023](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/10179427/)).

Crucially: all of this privacy formalization is *pen-and-paper* UC or game-based. I found no machine-checked proof of channel unlinkability anywhere.

### Proof-assistant ecosystems for crypto/game-hopping

**Isabelle: CryptHOL** is mature — game-based proofs with explicit intermediate games, failure events, indistinguishability-up-to reasoning, all checked by Isabelle ([J. Cryptology paper](https://people.inf.ethz.ch/basin/pubs/jcrypto19.pdf), [AFP tutorial](https://isa-afp.org/browser_info/current/AFP/Game_Based_Crypto/CryptHOL_Tutorial.html)), plus a Constructive Cryptography extension for composable statements ([CSF 2019](https://www.andreas-lochbihler.de/pub/lochbihler2019csf.pdf)). **Coq: SSProve** mechanizes state-separating proofs — modular "packages" composed algebraically plus a probabilistic relational program logic; fully formalized in Coq with case studies including sigma-protocol ZK ([eprint 2021/397](https://eprint.iacr.org/2021/397), [TOPLAS version](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3594735)). **EasyCrypt** (its own tool, same pRHL lineage) remains the workhorse for big game-hopping proofs like SHA-3 indifferentiability ([ref](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/337095885_Machine-Checked_Proofs_for_Cryptographic_Standards_Indifferentiability_of_Sponge_and_Secure_High-Assurance_Implementations_of_SHA-3)). **Lean 4**: mathlib itself has no game-based crypto framework; the gap is being filled by **VCV-io** (oracle effects/handlers for probabilistic adversaries and game-hopping, [eprint 2026/899](https://eprint.iacr.org/2026/899.pdf), [repo](https://github.com/Verified-zkEVM/VCV-io)) and **ArkLib** (verified SNARK components: IORs, sum-check, FRI/STIR/WHIR, Fiat-Shamir/BCS, [lean-lang.org writeup](https://lean-lang.org/use-cases/arklib/)), with the earlier student-scale [cryptolib](https://project-archive.inf.ed.ac.uk/msc/20215368/msc_proj.pdf) proving ElGamal semantic security in Lean 3.

## Relevance to our egress fleet

**1. The "ZK API Usage Credits = zk payment channel bound to one recipient" claim is historically accurate, and the canonical citation is Bolt.** Bolt's unidirectional channel is exactly a prepaid, ZK-refreshed credit balance with one recipient, where individual spends are unlinkable to each other and to the channel-open even from the recipient's own view ([eprint 2016/701](https://eprint.iacr.org/2016/701)). If we go with option (b), we are building Bolt-shaped unidirectional channels per member-gateway pair, and we should steal its security decomposition: (i) balance safety for the gateway (member can't spend more than escrowed), (ii) payment unlinkability for the member, (iii) abort/penalty handling for interrupted updates. Bolt's known pain points map to ours: channels must be *funded anonymously* or the open leaks identity, and per-pair channels fragment capital across N gateways — that's the real cost row in the table above.

**2. The capital-fragmentation problem of option (b) is exactly what the hub literature solved, and the hub literature is where the unlinkability-vs-infrastructure definitions live.** A member holding one funding relationship and paying N mutually distrusting gateways *through it* is structurally a payment channel hub. A2L+/BCS ([2022/942](https://eprint.iacr.org/2022/942.pdf)) and BlindHub ([2022/1735](https://eprint.iacr.org/2022/1735)) formally define unlinkability where the adversary is the infrastructure operator itself — precisely our threat model (gateways must not link requests to members, and gateways don't trust each other). Before inventing definitions for the fleet, take relationship anonymity from AMHL ([2018/472](https://eprint.iacr.org/2018/472.pdf)) and blindness/unlinkability from BCS; they are the field's vetted vocabulary.

**3. The A2L episode is the single most important cautionary tale for us.** A peer-reviewed S&P 2021 unlinkable-hub protocol had a definitional gap that admitted *completely insecure* instantiations satisfying the stated definitions ([2022/942](https://eprint.iacr.org/2022/942.pdf)). Privacy definitions for payment protocols are subtle in ways balance definitions are not. Opinion: if we hand-roll a zk-credit scheme, we should either (a) instantiate a published, repaired abstraction (BCS/A2L+) rather than designing our own game, or (b) budget for external cryptographic review of the definitions themselves, not just the implementation.

**4. What formal verification can realistically buy us, ranked.** (i) *Cheap and proven*: a TLA+ model of the member-gateway credit state machine (issue, spend, nullifier check, close, dispute). Grundmann-Hartenstein showed channel-level and network-level reasoning decompose cleanly and channel protocols are model-checkable at realistic scope ([2505.15568](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.15568)); our protocol is simpler than Lightning (unidirectional, no HTLC routing). This catches state-machine bugs — historically where channel implementations actually break — for days of effort, not years. (ii) *Medium*: mechanized balance-security proof in Why3 following [2503.07200](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.07200), or SSProve/EasyCrypt at the crypto level. (iii) *Frontier*: machine-checked *unlinkability*. Nobody has done this for any channel construction as far as I could find — every privacy proof in this literature is pen-and-paper. If we wanted it, SSProve ([2021/397](https://eprint.iacr.org/2021/397)) is the best-aligned framework (it already mechanizes sigma-protocol ZK), with CryptHOL/Constructive-Cryptography the Isabelle alternative. Lean is attractive because ArkLib/VCV-io ([lean-lang.org](https://lean-lang.org/use-cases/arklib/), [2026/899](https://eprint.iacr.org/2026/899.pdf)) are verifying exactly the SNARK components (Fiat-Shamir, sum-check, FRI) a Semaphore-adjacent stack rests on — but its game-based protocol layer is young; do not plan a channel-privacy proof in Lean this year. Mathlib itself gives you no crypto games.

**5. On option (a), the distributed spent set.** The channel literature has little to say about it directly — a shared spent set across N gateways is e-cash-style double-spend detection plus a replication problem, not a channel. Note though that the Lightning UC treatment's dependence on ledger liveness ([2019/778](https://eprint.iacr.org/2019/778.pdf)) reappears in disguise: a "distributed spent set" is a small consensus/broadcast layer among mutually distrusting gateways, and its consistency guarantees would carry the same load the ledger carries in every channel proof. If gateways can partition or equivocate, double-spend safety degrades exactly like channel safety degrades with a bad ledger. That's an argument *for* the channel shape (b): it needs no gateway-to-gateway trust or shared state at all, which the whole UC channel literature demonstrates is provable under minimal assumptions.
