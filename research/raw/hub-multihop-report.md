## The constructions

### TumbleBit

TumbleBit is a unidirectional payment hub. In the on-chain Escrow Phase, each payer Alice opens a 2-of-2 escrow with the Tumbler holding Q coins, and, critically, the Tumbler opens a 2-of-2 escrow with each payee Bob, itself escrowing Q coins toward that payee ([paper, Sec. III](https://www.ndss-symposium.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/09/ndss201701-3HeilmanPaper.pdf)). Payments are fixed-denomination (1 unit each; the anonymity properties *require* all payments to have the same denomination, per Sec. III-A of the paper). A payment is an off-chain puzzle exchange: the Tumbler gives Bob a "puzzle-promise" (an ECDSA signature on a cash-out transaction, encrypted under a key that is the solution to an RSA puzzle z). Bob blinds z with a random RSA blinding factor and hands the blinded puzzle to Alice; Alice runs a fair-exchange "puzzle-solver" protocol with the Tumbler (cut-and-choose over m real + n fake puzzles, so a cheating Tumbler succeeds with probability 1/C(m+n,n)), pays 1 coin per solution, and returns the solution to Bob, who unblinds it and can now claim 1 coin from the Tumbler's escrow. In the Cash-Out Phase everyone closes: Bob claims Q' coins matching his Q' solved puzzles; Alice signs a cash-out reflecting j payments made; the blockchain accepts only one cash-out per escrow, which is what prevents double-spending (all intermediate T_cash(A,T)(i) point to the same escrow output, so only one confirms).

The anonymity definition is explicit and worth internalizing: the Tumbler's view is the set of escrows, the timed puzzle-solver interactions with payers, and cash-outs; unlinkability requires that all "compatible interaction multi-graphs" (assignments of payments to payer-payee pairs consistent with that view) be equally likely. The result is k-anonymity within an epoch, where k is the number of payments *successfully completed* in that epoch, not the hub's whole clientele. The paper itself flags the caveats: payees get strictly better anonymity than payers (the Tumbler sees the time of each payer interaction but only aggregate payee cash-outs); an abort by the Tumbler on Alice's payment cuts k to k-1 (mitigated by payees using fresh ephemeral addresses per epoch and discarding suspicious payments); and the notion "suffers from" cross-epoch intersection attacks, which the authors acknowledge citing prior mix literature ([paper, Sec. III-C, IV-A, and Remark: Intersection attacks](https://www.ndss-symposium.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/09/ndss201701-3HeilmanPaper.pdf)). Capital: the Tumbler's locked capital scales as Q per payee channel for the epoch, i.e. roughly the total expected payment volume, and payers pay fees for both escrow transactions to resist DoS/Sybil (Sec. III "DoS and Sybil protection").

### A2L (and the A2L+ repair)

A2L keeps TumbleBit's two-protocol shape (puzzle promise with the receiver, puzzle solver with the sender) but replaces RSA puzzles + cut-and-choose with two primitives: adaptor signatures (the hub's "incomplete signature" on a tx paying Bob can only be completed with secret k, and completing it reveals k to the hub) and a randomizable puzzle, which is the adaptor condition C = k·G paired with an additively homomorphic encryption of k under the hub's own key ([Oakland slides, pp. 13-14](https://erkantairi.com/slides/a2l-oakland.pdf)). Flow: hub gives Bob a promise (adaptor signature on tx_G) plus puzzle Π; Bob randomizes it with r_B and forwards to Alice; Alice re-randomizes with r_A and runs puzzle-solving with the hub, which decrypts using its trapdoor and, in completing Alice's payment signature, reveals r_A·r_B·k to Alice; Alice strips r_A, sends r_B·k to Bob; Bob strips r_B and completes the hub's signature paying him. The hub decrypted its own key blinded twice, so it cannot correlate the two sessions. Everything needed on-chain is signature verification + timelocks, hence the interoperability claim (works even for "scriptless" chains, e.g. Monero, per the slides' conclusion), and the measured cost is ~9.92 KB per payment versus TumbleBit's 327 KB, roughly 33x less bandwidth ([slides p. 16](https://erkantairi.com/slides/a2l-oakland.pdf); TumbleBit's 327 KB figure from [the NDSS paper, Sec. I-A](https://www.ndss-symposium.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/09/ndss201701-3HeilmanPaper.pdf)).

Two design points matter for us. First, griefing: TumbleBit lets an attacker request many promises (each backed by locked hub coins) and never redeem them; A2L adds a privacy-preserving registration protocol in which the sender pays first and receives a randomizable registration token, whose re-randomized form the receiver spends to start a puzzle-promise, so promise-issuance is pre-paid and unlinkable ([slides pp. 8, 15](https://erkantairi.com/slides/a2l-oakland.pdf)). Second, the formal-security caution: Glaeser et al. (CCS 2022) showed the security/privacy model of the S&P 2021 paper was flawed, gave concrete attacks and counterexamples against A2L as specified, defined blind conditional signatures (BCS) as the right abstraction for "coin mixing services," and proposed A2L+ (and a UC-secure variant A2L-UC) satisfying the fixed definitions ([abstract](https://eprint.iacr.org/2022/942.pdf), [ACM page](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3548606.3560637)). If you build anything A2L-shaped, build A2L+, not the 2021 spec.

### AMHL (anonymous multi-hop locks)

AMHL addresses the multi-hop path case (Lightning-style: sender -> intermediary -> ... -> receiver) rather than a designated hub. The starting observation is that Lightning's HTLC construction reuses the same hash condition along the whole path, so all intermediaries on a payment path can trivially collude to link it, and worse: the authors discovered the wormhole attack, where two colluding intermediaries on a path exclude the honest ones between them and steal their fees ([NDSS page](https://www.ndss-symposium.org/ndss-paper/anonymous-multi-hop-locks-for-blockchain-scalability-and-interoperability/), [eprint](https://eprint.iacr.org/2018/472.pdf)). AMHL is the primitive that fixes both: each hop's lock condition is a fresh randomization of the previous one (the sender sets up per-hop secrets), so conditions on adjacent hops are unlinkable, and releasing the right-hand lock is what lets each intermediary open its left-hand lock, which structurally prevents the wormhole skip. They give a generic construction from homomorphic one-way functions for scripting chains and a scriptless ECDSA-based construction; operations run in under 100 ms with under 500 bytes of communication, and the paper reports Lightning developers moving to adopt the ECDSA-based locks after disclosure ([NDSS page](https://www.ndss-symposium.org/ndss-paper/anonymous-multi-hop-locks-for-blockchain-scalability-and-interoperability/)).

The anonymity property is relationship anonymity against corrupted intermediaries: colluding intermediaries separated by at least one honest node cannot tell which sender-receiver pair a payment belongs to among concurrent payments. Note this is anonymity against *path insiders*; the sender still chooses and knows the path, and the first intermediary still knows the sender's channel identity. A hub (one-intermediary path) is the degenerate case: AMHL's randomization is exactly what A2L industrializes for the hub topology with the added fair-exchange economics.

### BlindHub and Accio (the variable-amount generation)

Every Bitcoin-compatible PCH with relationship anonymity before 2023 (TumbleBit, A2L) required fixed denominations, because the amount itself is a linking tag; you had to run parallel instances per denomination ([BlindHub abstract](https://eprint.iacr.org/2022/1735)). BlindHub (S&P 2023) fixes this with blind adaptor signatures (adaptor signing over a blind signature scheme, so the tumbler signs payment transactions without seeing them) plus flexible blind conditional signatures, and a BlindChannel where one party cannot see channel balances; the tumbler can neither link sender to receiver nor use the amount to do it ([Monash summary](https://research.monash.edu/en/publications/blindhub-bitcoin-compatible-privacy-preserving-payment-channel-hu), [IEEE](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/10179427/)).

Accio (CCS 2023) gets variable amounts and unlinkability by a different, cheaper route: it abandons the puzzle-promise/puzzle-solver paradigm entirely and hides state on only one side, keeping the payer-tumbler channel in plaintext while hiding the tumbler-payee channel state from the tumbler, with no NIZKs. Because the tumbler never locks coins in advance to facilitate a payment, the griefing attack disappears by construction, and the authors report better computation and communication than prior unlinkable PCHs ([eprint](https://eprint.iacr.org/2023/1326), [ACM](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3576915.3616577)). The asymmetry ("payer side plaintext, payee side hidden") mirrors TumbleBit's observation that payees inherently get better anonymity than payers.

### Bolt (the "one channel, one recipient" ancestor)

Bolt (Green-Miers, CCS 2017) is the closest academic ancestor of "a zk payment channel bound to one recipient." A customer and merchant maintain a channel whose state is a blind commitment; each payment updates the committed balance in zero knowledge, and the merchant learns nothing per-payment beyond "a valid payment of amount X occurred on *some* channel of mine" (payments on the same channel are mutually unlinkable, which is stronger than Lightning, where the counterparty sees the whole channel history). Double-spending of a channel state is punished at channel close via revocation tokens. Bolt also sketches a hub variant for paying merchants through an untrusted intermediary, but it requires a currency with strong on-chain anonymity (Zcash-style) to close channels without linking them ([eprint](https://eprint.iacr.org/2016/701), [ACM](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3133956.3134093), [analysis](https://blog.acolyer.org/2017/12/13/bolt-anonymous-payment-channels-for-decentralized-currencies-part-i/)).

Direct answer to the framing question: in none of these systems is the anonymity set "the hub's whole clientele." It is always the set of *concurrent, protocol-compatible* payments (same epoch, and for TumbleBit/A2L same denomination), degraded by aborts and by cross-epoch intersection ([TumbleBit Sec. IV-A and Remark on intersection attacks](https://www.ndss-symposium.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/09/ndss201701-3HeilmanPaper.pdf)). Clientele size is an upper bound you will not reach.

## Relevance to our egress fleet

**Our topology is not actually the PCH topology, and that matters.** TumbleBit/A2L/BlindHub hide *who is paying whom* through a hub that is neither payer nor payee. In our fleet, the gateway is the payee: the member pays the gateway for egress service. There is no third party to hide. So "one gateway = one hub" is the wrong mapping for the core payment; the right ancestor for "zk payment channel bound to one recipient" is Bolt, not TumbleBit. The claim that ZK API Usage Credits is "fundamentally a zk payment channel bound to one recipient" checks out structurally: Bolt is precisely a zk-committed balance with one designated recipient, per-payment unlinkability against that recipient, and double-spend handling local to the channel ([Bolt](https://eprint.iacr.org/2016/701)). Its known pain points transfer too: aborts/timing as linking vectors and the need for anonymous funding/closing so channel endpoints don't deanonymize (Bolt leaned on Zcash for this; our analogue is funding credits via the Semaphore identity without linking the funding event to spends).

**Where the PCH literature does bite: cross-gateway unlinkability and the "distributed spent set" question.** Candidate (b), a zk channel per member-gateway pair, localizes double-spend prevention: the channel state IS the spent set, maintained pairwise, no gateway coordination needed, exactly as TumbleBit's escrow admits only one cash-out ([TumbleBit Sec. V-C](https://www.ndss-symposium.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/09/ndss201701-3HeilmanPaper.pdf)) and Bolt punishes stale states via revocation. That is the strongest argument against candidate (a): a shared spent set across N mutually distrusting gateways is only *necessary* in a bearer-token (Chaumian ecash) design where a credit minted anywhere can be spent anywhere; channels make double-spending a pairwise problem and remove the replicated-state surface entirely. The costs of the channel model are the mirror image: capital/credit fragmentation per pair (M members x N gateways channels; TumbleBit's hub had to escrow Q per counterparty per epoch, and our members would prepay per gateway) and anonymity-set fragmentation, since each gateway sees its own channel population, and cross-gateway traffic correlation is an intersection attack the TumbleBit authors already warned about within one hub.

**If members should buy credit once and spend at any gateway, that is exactly an A2L+ instance.** Member holds a channel/balance at gateway A, wants to spend at gateway B without A or B linking the two: run A2L+-style blind conditional signatures with A as tumbler (member "pays themselves" across gateways), or with B as tumbler. Use A2L+ (CCS 2022 definitions), not A2L-2021, given the demonstrated proof gaps ([Foundations of Coin Mixing](https://eprint.iacr.org/2022/942.pdf)). Also copy two specific mechanisms regardless of shape: A2L's pre-paid randomizable registration token as the anti-griefing gate (it is structurally identical to an RLN-gated request ticket, and composes naturally with our epoch nullifiers), and Accio's asymmetry insight that you only need to hide state on the side you care about, which for us means the gateway can keep a plaintext per-epoch counter while the member's identity-to-balance link stays hidden ([Accio](https://eprint.iacr.org/2023/1326)).

**Denominations and epochs are the anonymity budget.** Fixed-denomination requests (1 credit = 1 request, which we already have via RLN) put us in the TumbleBit/A2L regime where amount is not a linking tag, and our anonymity set per epoch is "requests completed that epoch at that gateway," not "members." Design consequences: keep denominations single-valued (do NOT add variable pricing per request unless we adopt BlindHub-style blind amounts), expect aborted/failed requests to shrink the set exactly as TumbleBit's k = N-1 abort analysis says, and treat cross-epoch intersection as the primary long-run deanonymization channel, which argues for coarse epochs and padding/decoy traffic at low-utilization gateways.
